The Bhagavad Gita on Inner Peace - Equanimity in the Storm

Krishna teaches Arjuna that true peace comes not from controlling external circumstances, but from mastering the mind through equanimity, detachment, and selfless action.

Overview

The Bhagavad Gita (“Song of God”) is a 700-verse dialogue between Krishna (Supreme Being in human form) and Arjuna (warrior prince) set on a battlefield where Arjuna must fight against his own relatives, teachers, and friends in a war over rightful kingship.

Arjuna, paralyzed by moral anguish, asks: “How can I kill these people? What peace can I find in such violence?”

Krishna’s response contains one of world literature’s most profound teachings on peace: True peace doesn’t come from avoiding conflict or achieving perfect external conditions—it comes from inner equilibrium (samatva) regardless of circumstances.

This teaching has inspired everyone from Mahatma Gandhi to Martin Luther King Jr., offering a path to peace amid unavoidable challenges.

Sacred Text

From Bhagavad Gita Chapter 2 (Verses 47-48)

कर्मण्येवाधिकारस्ते मा फलेषु कदाचन। मा कर्मफलहेतुर्भुर्मा ते सङ्गोऽस्त्वकर्मणि॥

Karmaṇy-evādhikāras te mā phaleṣhu kadāchana Mā karma-phala-hetur bhūr mā te saṅgo’stvakarmaṇi

योगस्थः कुरु कर्माणि सङ्गं त्यक्त्वा धनञ्जय। सिद्ध्यसिद्ध्योः समो भूत्वा समत्वं योग उच्यते॥

Yogasthaḥ kuru karmāṇi saṅgaṃ tyaktvā dhanañjaya Siddhy-asiddhyoḥ samo bhūtvā samatvaṃ yoga uchyate

Translation (Eknath Easwaran)

You have the right to work, but never to the fruit of work.

You should never engage in action for the sake of reward,

Nor should you long for inaction.

Perform work in this world, Arjuna, as one established in yoga (union),

Abandoning selfish attachments, and alike in success and defeat.

For yoga is perfect evenness of mind.

— Bhagavad Gita 2:47-48

From Chapter 2 (Verses 54-55): The State of Stable Wisdom

अर्जुन उवाच स्थितप्रज्ञस्य का भाषा समाधिस्थस्य केशव। स्थितधीः किं प्रभाषेत किमासीत व्रजेत किम्॥

Arjuna uvācha Sthita-prajñasya kā bhāṣhā samādhi-sthasya keśhava Sthita-dhīḥ kiṃ prabhāṣheta kim āsīta vrajeta kim

श्रीभगवानुवाच प्रजहाति यदा कामान्सर्वान्पार्थ मनोगतान्। आत्मन्येवात्मना तुष्टः स्थितप्रज्ञस्तदोच्यते॥

Śhrī-bhagavān uvācha Prajahāti yadā kāmān sarvān pārtha mano-gatān Ātmany-evātmanā tuṣhṭaḥ sthita-prajñas tadochyate

Translation (Eknath Easwaran)

Arjuna: “Tell me, Krishna, about those who live in wisdom, ever aware of the Self. How do they talk? How sit? How move about?”

Krishna: “They live in wisdom who see themselves in all and all in them, who have renounced every selfish desire and sense craving tormenting the heart.”

“Neither agitated by grief nor hankering after pleasure, they live free from lust and fear and anger. Established in meditation, they are truly wise.”

— Bhagavad Gita 2:54-55

Meaning & Interpretation

For Beginners

What is Krishna teaching about peace?

Krishna reveals that peace is not a destination—it’s a state of mind you can cultivate anywhere, anytime, even on a battlefield (literally and metaphorically).

Three Keys to Inner Peace:

1. Karma Yoga (Action Without Attachment)

“You have the right to work, but never to the fruit of work” (2:47)

This means:

  • Do your duty, give your best effort
  • But don’t be attached to results
  • Success or failure—remain balanced
  • Your job: do the work. Universe’s job: provide the results.

Example: Student takes exam:

  • With attachment: “I must get an A or I’m worthless!” (anxiety, fear, peace destroyed)
  • Without attachment: “I studied well. Whatever result comes, I accept.” (peace maintained)

2. Samatva (Equanimity)

“Established in yoga, perform work…alike in success and defeat. For yoga is perfect evenness of mind” (2:48)

Samatva (समत्व) = Equilibrium, evenness, balance

Not:

  • Indifference (not caring)
  • Suppression (pretending you don’t feel)
  • Passivity (not trying)

But:

  • Acceptance of what is, while working towards what should be
  • Inner stability amid outer turbulence
  • Choosing how you respond vs. reacting

3. Sthita-prajna (Stable Wisdom)

“They live in wisdom…neither agitated by grief nor hankering after pleasure” (2:55-56)

Sthita-prajna (स्थितप्रज्ञ) = One of stable wisdom/intelligence

Characteristics:

  • Not dependent on external conditions for happiness
  • Self-contained joy (ātmany-evātmanā tuṣhṭaḥ)
  • Free from compulsive desires
  • Anchored in the eternal Self (Atman)

Why does this bring peace?

Because lack of peace comes from:

  • Craving for pleasure (I’ll be happy when…)
  • Aversion to pain (I’ll be miserable if…)
  • Identifying with temporary outcomes
  • Forgetting your eternal nature

Krishna’s solution: Remember your true Self (Atman), do your duty, stay balanced. Peace follows naturally.

Historical Context

The Setting: Kurukshetra Battlefield

Two branches of the same family (Pandavas vs. Kauravas) are about to war over the throne. Arjuna, greatest warrior of his age, sees:

  • His grandfather (Bhishma)
  • His teacher (Drona)
  • His cousins (Kauravas)
  • His friends and allies

All lined up to kill or be killed by him.

He breaks down:

“My limbs fail, my mouth is parched, my body trembles… I see no good in killing my kinsmen. I desire neither victory nor kingdom nor pleasures.” (1:28-32)

Arjuna’s dilemma is universal:

  • How to do what’s necessary when it causes pain?
  • How to find peace amid unavoidable conflict?
  • How to act righteously when all choices seem wrong?

Krishna’s Teaching Timeline:

  • Chapter 1: Arjuna’s despair
  • Chapter 2: Fundamental teaching (karma yoga, sankhya, self-knowledge)
  • Chapters 3-18: Elaboration and deepening

Chapter 2 is the foundation - if you understand it, the rest follows.

Date & Composition:

The Gita is part of the Mahabharata epic (longest poem in history):

  • Written: 400 BCE - 400 CE (gradual composition)
  • Set in: Ancient India (date unclear, possibly 3102 BCE according to tradition)
  • Core teaching: Likely Buddha’s contemporary or slightly later

Classical Commentary

Adi Shankaracharya (8th century CE)

Founder of Advaita Vedanta, Shankara commented:

“Samatva (equanimity) is yoga because it is the means of union with the Supreme. When the mind is free from the pairs of opposites—heat and cold, pleasure and pain, praise and blame—it naturally turns inward to its source, the Atman. This is the highest yoga.”

Ramanuja (11th century CE)

Founder of Vishishtadvaita, Ramanuja taught:

“Karma yoga is not merely mechanical action. It is action dedicated to God (Bhagavad), performed as worship, with detachment to fruits. The Lord accepts such work and grants the devotee steady wisdom and ultimately moksha (liberation).”

Swami Vivekananda (19th century)

“The Gita teaches that you can be in the world but not of it. Do your duty, fight your battles, earn your living—but keep your mind fixed on the Eternal. This is practical spirituality for modern life, not running away to caves.”

Modern Interpretation

Dr. S. Radhakrishnan (Philosopher-President of India)

“The Gita is not a call to war but a call to spiritual awakening. Arjuna’s battlefield is every human’s life situation—full of duties, conflicts, and challenges. Krishna teaches not escape but engagement with detachment. You cannot avoid action; you can only purify your motivation and balance your response.”

Mahatma Gandhi

Gandhi called the Gita his “spiritual dictionary” and reinterpreted the battlefield symbolically:

“The Gita is not a historical text but an allegory. The ‘war’ is the eternal conflict between good and evil within each person. Krishna’s teaching: Fight your lower nature with your higher nature, using the weapons of truth, nonviolence, and self-control. Ahimsa (nonviolence) is my Gita’s central message.”

(Note: Gandhi’s interpretation is controversial—many scholars see the Gita as accepting warfare under dharmic conditions.)

Swami Satchidananda (Integral Yoga)

“Yoga is peace. The Gita defines yoga as ‘samatva’—equanimity. When your mind is like a still lake, the Divine reflects perfectly in it. Disturbance comes from attachment—‘I want this, I don’t want that.’ Practice seeing God in all outcomes, and peace becomes your natural state.”

Advanced Analysis

The Philosophy of Karma Yoga

Karma (कर्म) = Action, work, deed Yoga (योग) = Union, discipline, skillful action

Karma Yoga = The path of selfless action

Three Key Principles:

1. Nishkama Karma (Desireless Action)

  • Not: Doing nothing
  • But: Acting without ego-driven desires
  • Motivation: Duty (dharma), service, love—not personal gain

2. Yajna Bhava (Spirit of Sacrifice)

  • All action offered to the Divine
  • “I am the instrument; God is the doer”
  • Transforms work into worship

3. Prasada Buddhi (Acceptance of Results)

  • Whatever comes is “grace” (prasad)
  • Trust in divine intelligence
  • No complaint, no arrogance

Result: Action without creating binding karma (samskara). Freedom in action.

Samatva as Yoga

“Samatvaṃ yoga uchyate” - “Evenness of mind is called yoga”

This is a radical definition of yoga:

  • Not fancy postures (though asanas help)
  • Not mystical experiences (though they may arise)
  • But: Steady, balanced mind in all situations

Test of yoga: Can you remain peaceful when things go “wrong”?

The Gunas and Peace

Hindu philosophy analyzes all nature through three gunas (qualities):

  1. Sattva (सत्त्व) - Purity, balance, light, wisdom
  2. Rajas (रजस्) - Activity, passion, restlessness, desire
  3. Tamas (तमस्) - Inertia, darkness, ignorance, lethargy

Peace requires cultivating sattva:

  • Pure food, thoughts, company
  • Meditation, study, service
  • Rising above rajas (agitation) and tamas (dullness)
  • Ultimately: Transcending all three to pure consciousness (turiya)

Atman vs. Ego (Prakriti)

The Gita distinguishes:

  • Atman (आत्मन्) = True Self, pure consciousness, eternal, unchanging
  • Ahamkara (अहंकार) = Ego, false self, temporary, identifies with body-mind

Peace comes from identifying with Atman:

  • “I am not this body, not these emotions, not these thoughts”
  • “I am the witness, the eternal Self, one with Brahman”
  • “Success/failure touch my body, not my essence”

This is jnana (wisdom) aspect of the Gita’s teaching.

Modern Application

Personal Practice: Cultivating Equanimity

Daily Samatva Practice:

Morning (Set Intention):

"Today, whatever happens—success or failure, praise or blame, pleasure or pain—I will remain balanced. I am not these temporary events. I am the eternal witness."

Throughout Day (Pause and Balance):

When something “good” happens:

  • Enjoy it, but don’t cling: “This too shall pass”
  • Gratitude without attachment
  • Don’t let it inflate ego

When something “bad” happens:

  • Feel it, but don’t drown: “This too shall pass”
  • Acceptance without resignation
  • Don’t let it crush self-worth

Evening (Reflect):

  • Where did I lose equanimity today?
  • What triggered attachment/aversion?
  • How can I respond better tomorrow?

The Gita’s Formula:

Stimulus → Pause → Choice → Response
(Not: Stimulus → Automatic Reaction)

Viktor Frankl (Holocaust survivor, psychiatrist) said:

“Between stimulus and response there is a space. In that space is our power to choose our response. In our response lies our growth and our freedom.”

This is exactly what Krishna teaches Arjuna.

Professional Application

Karma Yoga at Work:

Old mindset (attached):

  • “I must get this promotion or I’m a failure”
  • “My worth depends on my boss’s opinion”
  • “If this project fails, I’m ruined”

Result: Anxiety, burnout, fear-based decisions

Karma yoga mindset (detached):

  • “I’ll do excellent work. Promotion comes or doesn’t—I’m okay”
  • “I value myself regardless of external validation”
  • “I’ll give my best. Universe handles results”

Result: Peace, better performance (not paralyzed by fear), sustainable career

Research confirms: People who practice “task orientation” (process) vs. “ego orientation” (results) perform better and report greater well-being.

Social Justice and the Gita

Gandhi’s Interpretation:

Gandhi saw karma yoga as foundation for nonviolent resistance (satyagraha):

  • Fight injustice (your dharma as human)
  • But don’t hate oppressors (they’re caught in ignorance)
  • Act without attachment to outcomes (you may be jailed, beaten, killed—act anyway)
  • Trust in truth’s ultimate victory (satyagraha = “truth-force”)

Gandhi’s calm in face of violence shocked opponents—he’d internalized the Gita’s teaching on equanimity.

Contemporary Application:

Activists burn out from:

  • Attachment to specific outcomes
  • Rage that consumes them
  • Despair when change is slow

Gita’s wisdom for activists:

  • Act from love, not hate
  • Do your part; don’t carry whole world
  • Trust the arc of justice (long, but it bends)
  • Sustain yourself for the marathon

Reflection Questions

For Personal Reflection:

  1. What am I currently attached to? How does this attachment disturb my peace?
  2. When did I last experience true equanimity—untouched by success or failure?
  3. What would change if I truly believed “I have the right to work, but not to the fruits”?
  4. How can I distinguish between healthy striving and anxious grasping?

For Group Discussion:

  1. Is detachment compatible with caring deeply about outcomes?
  2. Can you be politically engaged while practicing karma yoga?
  3. How does “stable wisdom” differ from suppressing emotions?
  4. What role does grace (Divine will) vs. effort play in finding peace?

A Prayer for Equanimity (Inspired by Gita)

Om Shanti Shanti Shanti

May I remember:

I am not this body, these emotions, these circumstances.

I am the eternal witness, the unchanging Self.

Success does not make me greater.

Failure does not make me lesser.

I am That which observes both.

May I act with full effort and zero attachment.

May I meet triumph and disaster

As the two impostors they are.

May I rest in the peace

That is my true nature.

Om Shanti Shanti Shanti

(Peace Peace Peace)
