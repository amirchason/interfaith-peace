Love Your Enemies - The Radical Command of Jesus

Jesus's command to love our enemies stands as one of Christianity's most challenging and transformative teachings, calling believers to reflect God's indiscriminate love.

Overview

“Love your enemies” - three words that have echoed through history, challenging, inspiring, and confounding followers of Jesus for two millennia. This teaching, found in the Sermon on the Mount, represents one of Christianity’s most radical ethical demands and its most distinctive contribution to moral philosophy.

Jesus doesn’t merely say “don’t hate your enemies” or “tolerate your enemies.” He commands active, sacrificial love for those who wish us harm. This isn’t natural. It’s supernatural. It’s the love of God made human practice.

Meaning & Interpretation

For Beginners

What Does “Love Your Enemies” Mean?

Jesus uses the Greek word agape (ἀγάπη) - not romantic love (eros) or friendship (philia), but deliberate goodwill and active care for another’s wellbeing, regardless of whether they “deserve” it.

Loving enemies means:

  1. Not seeking revenge - Breaking cycles of retaliation
  2. Praying for their good - Genuinely desiring their wellbeing
  3. Doing good to them - Active kindness, not just avoiding harm
  4. Blessing, not cursing - Speaking well of them, not spreading hatred
  5. Seeing their humanity - Recognizing they are made in God’s image too

What it does NOT mean:

  • ❌ Tolerating abuse or staying in dangerous situations
  • ❌ Pretending harm didn’t happen (denial)
  • ❌ Skipping grief, anger, or healing processes
  • ❌ Letting people face no consequences for their actions
  • ❌ Being a doormat or enabling destructive behavior

Why does Jesus command this?

“So that you may be children of your Father in heaven” (v.45)

God loves indiscriminately - “makes his sun rise on the evil and on the good.” When we love enemies, we reflect God’s nature. We become like our Father.

Historical Context

The Jewish Background

The Hebrew Bible commands:

“You shall love your neighbor as yourself” (Leviticus 19:18)

But who is the “neighbor”? Some Jewish interpreters limited it to fellow Jews. The phrase “hate your enemy” doesn’t appear in the Torah, but sectarian groups like the Essenes (Dead Sea Scrolls community) taught:

“Love all the sons of light… and hate all the sons of darkness” (Community Rule 1:9-10)

Jesus radically expands the definition of neighbor.

The Roman Occupation Context

Jesus spoke these words during brutal Roman occupation:

  • Roman soldiers could force Jews to carry their gear for one mile
  • Heavy taxation exploited the poor
  • Public crucifixions terrorized resisters
  • Jewish zealots advocated violent rebellion

Into this context, Jesus says: “Love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you.”

This was politically explosive:

  • Not collaboration (Jesus challenged Roman power structures)
  • Not violent resistance (rejected Zealot methods)
  • A “third way”: Active nonviolent resistance grounded in enemy love

Early Church Application

Early Christians took this literally:

  • Justin Martyr (2nd century): “We who hated and murdered one another… now live together and pray for our enemies.”
  • Tertullian (2nd-3rd century): “Christians are forbidden to hate even their enemies.”
  • Martyrs’ witness: Many early Christians died forgiving their executors, echoing Jesus on the cross: “Father, forgive them, for they know not what they do” (Luke 23:34)

Scholarly Commentary

Dr. Miroslav Volf (Yale Divinity School)

A Croatian theologian who lived through Balkan ethnic violence, Volf writes:

“The practice of nonviolence requires a belief in divine vengeance… It takes the quiet of a suburban home for the birth of the thesis that human nonviolence corresponds to God’s refusal to judge. In a scorched land—soaked in the blood of the innocent, the stench of evil filling the nostrils—one has only one choice: believe in the reality of the judgment of God or give up on justice altogether.”

Key insight: Enemy love is possible when we trust God’s justice, not our revenge.

Dr. Scot McKnight (Northern Seminary)

“Jesus isn’t creating a new law (‘You must love enemies’). He’s revealing what was always God’s heart. The Torah’s love command was never meant to have limits. Jesus tears down the walls we build around ‘neighbor’ and extends it to the whole human family—including those who want to destroy us.”

St. Augustine (4th-5th century)

“Love your enemies… Perhaps he is evil as an enemy, but he is good as a human being. Love what God made, not what the person himself has made. Love his nature, hate his sin.”

Augustine distinguishes: Hate the sin, love the sinner (though this phrase is often misused today).

Dietrich Bonhoeffer (20th century)

German pastor executed by Nazis for opposing Hitler:

“Loving your enemy means willing their good, not capitulating to their evil. Jesus’s enemy love is not weakness but the ultimate act of strength—refusing to let evil dictate the terms of your response.”

Advanced Analysis

The Grammar of Agape

Greek has four words for love:

  1. Eros - Romantic/sexual love
  2. Storge - Family affection
  3. Philia - Friendship love
  4. Agape - Deliberate, volitional goodwill

Jesus commands agape for enemies—not warm feelings, but willed action for their good. This is crucial: You can choose agape even when you feel nothing but hurt.

“Be Perfect” (τέλειοι, teleioi)

“Be perfect, therefore, as your heavenly Father is perfect” (v.48)

The Greek word teleioi means “complete,” “mature,” “whole”—not sinless perfection, but maturity in love. Luke’s parallel says: “Be merciful, as your Father is merciful” (Luke 6:36).

Perfect love = Complete, indiscriminate love like God’s.

The Structure of the Teaching

Jesus uses a rhetorical pattern:

  1. Old understanding: “You have heard it said…”
  2. New revelation: “But I say to you…”
  3. Divine standard: “Be perfect as your Father is perfect”

He’s not abolishing the law but revealing its ultimate intention: To form a people who mirror God’s character.

Theological Implications

1. Imago Dei (Image of God)

Even enemies bear God’s image (Genesis 1:27). To hate them is to hate what God made. Enemy love protects divine image in all humanity.

2. Grace, Not Merit

God’s love isn’t earned—“sun rises on evil and good, rain on righteous and unrighteous” (v.45). Our enemy love reflects this unmerited grace.

3. Kingdom Ethics

This isn’t advice for “getting along.” It’s the ethic of God’s Kingdom breaking into the world. Jesus embodies it on the cross: forgiving those killing him.

4. Transformation, Not Resignation

Enemy love doesn’t accept evil. It transforms evil through active goodness. Romans 12:21: “Do not be overcome by evil, but overcome evil with good.”

Modern Application

Personal Practice

1. Pray for Enemies Daily

Jesus specifically commands: “Pray for those who persecute you” (v.44)

How:

  • Name the person who hurt you
  • Ask God to bless them (even if it feels fake at first)
  • Pray for their healing, growth, peace
  • Ask God to change your heart toward them

This is transformative. You can’t genuinely pray for someone’s good and keep hating them.

2. Refuse to Gossip About Them

Speaking evil of enemies perpetuates hate. Instead:

  • Decline to participate in trash-talking
  • Speak truthfully about harm done, but without malice
  • Look for ways to speak well or stay silent

3. Look for Their Humanity

Exercise: Imagine your enemy as a child—innocent, vulnerable, loved by God. What pain made them who they are? This doesn’t excuse harm, but it opens space for compassion.

4. Do Good to Them

Practical acts:

  • If you have power over them, use it mercifully
  • If they need help you can provide, offer it
  • Find small ways to show unexpected kindness

Romans 12:20: “If your enemies are hungry, feed them; if they are thirsty, give them something to drink.”

Social Application

Civil Rights Movement

Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. built his entire nonviolent resistance strategy on Jesus’s enemy love:

“Returning hate for hate multiplies hate, adding deeper darkness to a night already devoid of stars. Darkness cannot drive out darkness; only light can do that. Hate cannot drive out hate; only love can do that.”

Key strategies:

  • Nonviolent direct action (sit-ins, marches)
  • Refusing to return violence with violence
  • Declaring love for segregationists while resisting segregation
  • Praying for Bull Connor, George Wallace, and others

Result: Moral authority that eventually won hearts and changed laws.

South African Truth and Reconciliation Commission

Archbishop Desmond Tutu applied enemy love to post-apartheid healing:

  • Victims faced perpetrators publicly
  • Truth-telling instead of revenge
  • Forgiveness (where victims chose) instead of retaliation
  • Ubuntu philosophy (“I am because we are”) + Christian enemy love

Not perfect, but transformative alternative to cycles of vengeance.

Amish Response to School Shooting (2006)

After a gunman killed five Amish girls in Nickel Mines, PA:

  • Amish community immediately forgave the shooter
  • Visited and comforted the shooter’s widow
  • Invited her to their children’s funerals
  • Set up fund for shooter’s family

The world was stunned. This is enemy love in action.

Contemporary Challenges

1. When Enemies Are Systems, Not Individuals

How do you “love” systemic racism, patriarchy, or economic exploitation?

Response:

  • Love the people trapped in oppressive systems (including oppressors)
  • Resist the system while caring for humans within it
  • Work for transformation, not destruction of persons

2. Protecting the Vulnerable

Does enemy love mean we can’t stop abusers, tyrants, or criminals?

No. Jesus’s teaching is about:

  • Motivation: Protect out of love for all, not hatred
  • Methods: Minimize harm, use least violence necessary
  • Goals: Restoration, not revenge

Example: Stopping a child abuser is an act of love for the child AND the abuser (preventing further soul corruption).

3. Trauma and Timing

You cannot love enemies while still in acute trauma.

First: Safety, healing, processing. Then: Enemy love becomes possible, even transformative.

Forgiveness is a journey, not a switch.

Reflection Questions

For Personal Reflection:

  1. Who are my “enemies” (people I struggle to love)?
  2. What wound or fear makes enemy love hard for me?
  3. Have I ever experienced someone loving me when I was their “enemy”? How did it feel?
  4. What would change if I truly prayed for my enemies’ wellbeing?

For Group Discussion:

  1. Is enemy love possible without faith in God’s justice and grace?
  2. How do we balance enemy love with protecting victims?
  3. Can enemy love be effective in politics and international relations?
  4. What’s the difference between enemy love and enabling harm?

A Prayer for Enemy Love

(Adapted from St. Francis)

Lord, make me an instrument of your peace.

Help me see my enemies as you see them—

Broken, beloved, bearing your image.

Give me courage to pray for their good,

Even when my heart resists.

Teach me to bless those who curse me,

To do good to those who hate me.

Not because they deserve it,

But because You first loved me

When I was Your enemy.

Transform my heart

That I may be a child of the Father,

Reflecting Your indiscriminate, scandalous, beautiful love.

Amen.
