Peace in Islam: Core Teachings, Principles & Practices from Quran & Hadith

Peace in Islam is not merely the absence of war—it is a comprehensive spiritual, social, and political framework rooted in the concept of Salaam (السلام), one of the 99 Names of Allah. From the daily greeting “As-salamu alaykum” (Peace be upon you) to the Quranic command to “incline to peace” even during conflict, Islam presents a holistic vision of peace that encompasses inner tranquility, social harmony, justice, and reconciliation.

This comprehensive guide explores Islamic peace teachings from the Quran and Hadith, examines the life of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ as a peacemaker, analyzes classical and contemporary Islamic scholarship, and provides practical applications for fostering peace in today’s world.

Peace in Islam has multiple interconnected dimensions:

  1. Spiritual Peace: Inner tranquility through submission to Allah (Islam itself means “peace through submission”)
  2. Social Peace: Harmony in communities through justice, compassion, and reconciliation
  3. Salaam: A divine attribute—Allah is As-Salaam (The Source of Peace), and peace is a fundamental Islamic value
  4. Greeting: Every Muslim exchange begins with “As-salamu alaykum” (Peace be upon you)
  5. Paradise: Called Dar as-Salaam (The Abode of Peace)—the ultimate destiny of believers

Islam teaches that true peace requires both inner spiritual transformation and outer social justice—you cannot have lasting peace without addressing oppression, poverty, and injustice.

Part I: Salaam — The Islamic Concept of Peace

1. Allah as As-Salaam (The Source of Peace)

The foundation of Islamic peace theology is that Allah Himself is As-Salaam—The Peace, The Source of Peace, The Perfection beyond all defects.

Quran 59:23

He is Allah, other than whom there is no deity, the Sovereign, the Pure, **the Peace**, the Bestower of Faith, the Overseer, the Exalted in Might, the Compeller, the Superior.

Theological Implications:

  1. Peace is Divine: Since Allah is As-Salaam, peace is not merely a human ideal but a divine attribute reflecting God’s nature
  2. All Peace Emanates from Allah: True peace—inner, social, political—originates from connection with the Source of Peace
  3. Believers Reflect Divine Peace: Muslims are called to embody this divine attribute in their character and actions
  4. Paradise is Peace: The ultimate abode (Dar as-Salaam) reflects the perfect peace of Allah’s presence

"As-Salaam means that Allah is free from all defects and imperfections, and He is the One who grants peace and security to His servants. The servant's portion of this name is to have a heart at peace, free from the turmoil of desires and the anxiety of this world."

Imam Al-Ghazali The Ninety-Nine Beautiful Names of God (11th century)

2. Islam: The Religion Named After Peace

The very name “Islam” (الإسلام) shares the same Arabic root as “Salaam” (السلام):

  • Root: S-L-M (س-ل-م)
  • Islam (إسلام): Submission, surrender (to Allah’s will)
  • Salaam (سلام): Peace, safety, security
  • Muslim (مسلم): One who submits and finds peace
  • Salam (سَلَم): Safety, soundness, wholeness

Linguistic Connection: Islam and peace are linguistically and theologically inseparable. Islam is peace through submission to Allah—when one surrenders ego, desires, and will to the Creator, inner peace (sakina) results.

Daily Practice: Every time you say or hear “As-salamu alaykum” (Peace be upon you), remember:

  • You’re invoking divine peace upon another person
  • You’re acknowledging Allah as the Source of all peace
  • You’re practicing Islam’s foundational greeting 5+ times daily
  • The Prophet ﷺ said spreading Salaam increases love among Muslims (Sahih Muslim 54)

3. Paradise: Dar as-Salaam (The Abode of Peace)

The Quran repeatedly calls Paradise Dar as-Salaam (دار السلام)—The Home of Peace:

Quran 6:127

For them will be **the Home of Peace** with their Lord.

Quran 10:25

And Allah invites to **the Home of Peace** and guides whom He wills to a straight path.

Eschatological Vision: Islam presents peace as humanity’s ultimate destiny—Paradise is the realm of complete peace where:

  • No conflict exists (Quran 15:47: “We removed whatever was in their hearts of resentment”)
  • Allah’s greeting is “Peace” (Salaam) (Quran 36:58)
  • Angels enter with greetings of peace (Quran 13:23-24)
  • Complete inner tranquility (sakina) is attained

Implication: If the ultimate goal is peace, Muslims must actively pursue peace in this world as preparation for the eternal home of peace.

Part II: The Quran’s Peace Framework

4. The Command to Incline Toward Peace ⭐ FOUNDATIONAL VERSE

Quran 8:61

**And if they incline to peace, then incline to it [also]** and rely upon Allah. Indeed, it is He who is the Hearing, the Knowing.

Context: Revealed after the Battle of Badr (624 CE), when Muslims had just achieved their first military victory. Despite military advantage, Allah commands accepting peace if the enemy offers it.

Classical Tafsir Insights:

"This verse is one of the clearest proofs that **Islam prioritizes peace over war**. When the enemy inclines to peace—even if Muslims have the upper hand militarily—they **must accept it**. War in Islam is defensive and reluctant, not a first option."

Ibn Kathir Tafsir Ibn Kathir (14th century)

"The command 'incline to it' (*fajnah*) is **obligatory**, not merely recommended. If the enemy seeks peace, Muslims are **required** to reciprocate. This is because peace allows for the spread of the message through dialogue rather than conflict."

Al-Qurtubi Tafsir Al-Qurtubi (13th century)

Key Principles:

  1. Peace is the default: War is permitted only in defense (Quran 2:190)
  2. Reciprocity: If they offer peace, accept peace
  3. Trust in Allah: “And rely upon Allah”—don’t reject peace out of fear of betrayal
  4. Even during war: This command applies even in active conflict

5. No Compulsion in Religion — Peace Through Freedom

Quran 2:256

**There shall be no compulsion in [acceptance of] the religion.** The right course has become clear from the wrong.

Significance: This verse establishes that forced conversion is prohibited in Islam. True faith must be freely chosen, not coerced.

Classical Commentary:

"This verse was revealed concerning the *Ansar* (Helpers of Medina) who wanted to force their children to accept Islam. Allah prohibited this, establishing that **faith cannot be imposed**—it must come from sincere conviction."

Imam Al-Tabari Tafsir Al-Tabari (9th century)

Modern Application: Religious freedom is not merely tolerance but active respect for conscience. Many contemporary Muslim scholars (e.g., Abdullahi An-Na’im, Khaled Abou El Fadl) argue this verse forms the basis for Islamic support of universal religious freedom.

6. Reconciliation: Making Peace Between Believers

Quran 49:10

**The believers are but brothers, so make settlement between your brothers.** And fear Allah that you may receive mercy.

Preceding Verse (Quran 49:9):

“And if two factions among the believers should fight, then make settlement between the two. But if one of them oppresses the other, then fight against the one that oppresses until it returns to the ordinance of Allah. And if it returns, then make settlement between them in justice and act justly. Indeed, Allah loves those who act justly.”

Conflict Resolution Framework:

  1. Immediate intervention: Don’t let conflicts fester—intervene to reconcile
  2. Restore justice: If one party oppresses, defend the oppressed
  3. Pursue reconciliation: Even after fighting, the goal is peace, not punishment
  4. Justice is essential: Peace without justice is not Islamic peace

Hadith on Reconciliation:

Prophet Muhammad ﷺ said: “Shall I not tell you something more excellent than fasting, prayer, and charity? It is putting things right between people. For enmity and malice tear up heavenly rewards by the roots.” — Sunan Abu Dawud 4919 (Sahih)

Practical Implication: Making peace between conflicting parties is considered more virtuous than individual acts of worship—reflecting Islam’s emphasis on social harmony.

7. Repel Evil with Good — Revolutionary Ethic

Quran 41:34

**Repel [evil] by that [deed] which is better**; and thereupon, the one whom between you and him is enmity [will become] as though he was a devoted friend.

Following Verse (41:35):

“But none is granted it except those who are patient, and none is granted it except one having a great portion [of good].”

Spiritual Principle: Instead of responding to evil with equal evil (the default human reaction), Islam calls believers to respond with something better—transforming enemies into friends through superior character.

Classical Interpretations:

  • Al-Qurtubi: “Respond to harshness with gentleness, to anger with forbearance, to insult with forgiveness.”
  • Ibn Abbas (companion of Prophet): “This means when someone treats you badly, respond with patience and forgiveness until they become your close friend.”

Prophetic Example:

  • The people of Ta’if stoned Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, yet he refused to curse them and instead prayed: “O Allah, guide my people, for they do not know” (Sahih Bukhari)
  • When he conquered Mecca (630 CE) after years of persecution, he granted general amnesty: “Go, you are free” (Ithlaqū, antum al-tulaqāʾ) (Sirah Ibn Hisham)

Part III: Prophet Muhammad ﷺ as Model Peacemaker

8. The Prophetic Mission: Mercy to All Creation

Quran 21:107

**And We have not sent you, [O Muhammad], except as a mercy to the worlds.**

Key Term: Rahmah (رحمة) = Mercy, compassion, loving-kindness

Scope: Not just mercy to Muslims, but to:

  • All humanity (الناس)
  • All creation (العالمين) — including animals, environment, future generations

"The Prophet ﷺ was a mercy in all aspects: his teachings brought mercy, his character exemplified mercy, his judgments were merciful, and even his military campaigns were conducted with unprecedented mercy for that era."

Imam Nawawi Sharh Sahih Muslim (13th century)

9. Historical Examples of Prophet’s Peacemaking

A. The Treaty of Hudaybiyyah (628 CE) — Choosing Peace Over War

Context: Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and 1,400 Muslims traveled to Mecca for pilgrimage (Umrah). The Quraysh blocked them and offered a treaty with harsh terms:

  • Muslims must return without pilgrimage (this year)
  • 10-year peace treaty
  • Any Qurayshi who converts to Islam and flees to Medina must be returned
  • Any Muslim who leaves Medina for Mecca can stay

Reaction: Many companions, including Umar ibn al-Khattab, protested—the terms seemed humiliating. Yet the Prophet accepted the treaty.

Quranic Verdict: Allah called it “a clear victory” (Quran 48:1: Innā fataḥnā laka fatḥan mubīnā)

Why it was a victory:

  1. Peace created space for Islam: During 10 years of peace, more people converted than in previous 20 years of conflict
  2. Legitimized Muslims: Quraysh recognized Muslims as equal treaty partners
  3. Saved lives: Prevented bloodshed in the sacred city
  4. Strategic wisdom: The Prophet prioritized long-term peace over short-term pride

Lesson: Sometimes accepting less-than-perfect terms for peace is wiser than pursuing military victory.

B. The Conquest of Mecca (630 CE) — Forgiveness Over Revenge

Context: After Quraysh violated the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah, Muslims marched to Mecca with 10,000 troops—an overwhelming force.

Prophet’s Commands:

  1. Minimize violence: Ordered only defensive fighting—no revenge killings
  2. Safety for all: Declared multiple safe zones (homes, Ka’bah, houses of Abu Sufyan)
  3. General amnesty: “Go, you are free” (Ithlaqū, antum al-tulaqāʾ)—no revenge against persecutors

Remarkable Forgiveness:

  • Hind bint Utbah: Mutilated the Prophet’s uncle Hamza’s body—forgiven
  • Wahshi: Killed Hamza—forgiven
  • Abu Sufyan: Led opposition for 20 years—forgiven and honored
  • Ikrimah ibn Abi Jahl: Staunch enemy—forgiven, later became a martyr for Islam

Historical Significance: The conquest of Mecca is unparalleled in military history for its restraint and mercy despite overwhelming power. Compare this to typical ancient conquests (sacking of cities, slavery, massacre).

"The conquest of Mecca was a unique event in military history. The Prophet could easily have exacted revenge for decades of persecution, but instead he established a precedent of **magnanimity in victory** that shocked his contemporaries."

Karen Armstrong Muhammad: A Prophet for Our Time (2006)

C. The Constitution of Medina (622 CE) — First Pluralistic Social Contract

Background: When Prophet Muhammad ﷺ migrated to Medina, he drafted a written constitution establishing a multi-religious city-state.

Key Provisions (from Sirah Ibn Ishaq):

  1. Religious freedom: “The Jews have their religion and the Muslims have theirs”
  2. Mutual defense: All citizens—Muslim, Jewish, pagan—must defend Medina together
  3. Justice for all: Disputes adjudicated fairly regardless of religion
  4. Peaceful coexistence: No forced conversion, no oppression
  5. One community (Ummah): Despite religious differences, all are citizens with rights

Historical Significance: This was revolutionary for 7th-century Arabia—a social contract based on citizenship, not religion or tribe.

Modern Relevance: Many Muslim scholars cite this as precedent for Islamic support of pluralistic democracies where Muslims and non-Muslims live as equal citizens.

10. Prophetic Hadith on Peace

Hadith #1: Peace as the Essence of Islam

Prophet Muhammad ﷺ was asked: “What is the best thing in Islam?” He replied: “To feed people and to greet with Salaam those you know and those you do not know.” — Sahih Bukhari 12, Sahih Muslim 39

Analysis: The Prophet defines Islam’s “best” practice as spreading peace (Salaam) universally—not just to friends but to strangers, Muslims and non-Muslims.

Hadith #2: The Muslim Who Doesn’t Harm Others

“The Muslim is the one from whose tongue and hand people are safe, and the believer is the one from whom people’s lives and wealth are safe.” — Sunan an-Nasa’i 4995 (Sahih)

Definition of a Muslim: Not defined by ritual alone, but by creating safety and peace for others—no harm from your words (tongue) or actions (hand).

Hadith #3: The Believer Who Pursues Peace

“The believer is not one who eats his fill while his neighbor goes hungry.” — Al-Adab Al-Mufrad 112 (Authenticated by Albani)

Holistic Peace: True peace requires addressing economic injustice—you cannot have peace while neighbors starve.

Hadith #4: Forbidden to Harm Others

“There should be neither harming nor reciprocating harm.” (Lā ḍarara wa lā ḍirār) — Sunan Ibn Majah 2340 (Sahih)

Legal Principle: This hadith is foundational in Islamic jurisprudence (fiqh)—all actions that cause harm (ḍarar) to others are prohibited. This includes:

  • Physical harm
  • Psychological harm (insults, slander)
  • Economic harm (fraud, exploitation)
  • Environmental harm (polluting shared resources)

Hadith #5: Greeting of Peace Increases Love

“You will not enter Paradise until you believe, and you will not believe until you love one another. Shall I not tell you something that, if you did it, you would love one another? Spread Salaam amongst yourselves.” — Sahih Muslim 54

Social Cohesion: The simple act of saying “As-salamu alaykum” builds love, trust, and community bonds—the foundation of social peace.

Part IV: Justice as the Foundation of Peace

11. No Peace Without Justice

Islam rejects “negative peace” (mere absence of war) and insists on “positive peace” (presence of justice, equity, dignity).

Quran 4:135

**O you who have believed, be persistently standing firm in justice**, witnesses for Allah, even if it be against yourselves or parents or relatives.

Quran 5:8

**And do not let the hatred of a people prevent you from being just. Be just; that is nearer to righteousness.**

Revolutionary Principle: Islam commands justice even toward those you hate. Personal feelings must not compromise justice.

Historical Example:

  • Jewish Theft Case: A Jewish man and Muslim man disputed ownership of a shield. Witnesses testified against the Jew, but Caliph Ali investigated and found the Muslim witnesses were lying. Ali ruled in favor of the Jewish man despite community pressure. (Sirah Umar ibn al-Khattab)

12. Economic Justice for Social Peace

Quran 59:7

**So that wealth will not be a perpetual distribution among the rich from among you.**

Context: This verse establishes principles for distributing war spoils and state wealth—ensuring it doesn’t concentrate only among the wealthy.

Islamic Economic Justice Tools:

  1. Zakat (Mandatory Charity): 2.5% annual wealth tax redistributed to poor, needy, debtors
  2. Prohibition of Riba (Usury/Interest): Prevents exploitation through predatory lending
  3. Inheritance Laws: Prevent wealth concentration by distributing to multiple heirs
  4. Waqf (Endowments): Charitable trusts for public benefit (hospitals, schools, water)

Prophetic Teaching:

“Wealth is not diminished by charity. Allah increases the servant who forgives, and no one humbles himself for Allah’s sake except that Allah raises him.” — Sahih Muslim 2588

"**Oppression is the corruption of existence, and justice is the order of existence.** Through justice, the heavens and earth are established. Even a just state that permits unbelief may endure, but an oppressive state—even if Muslim—will collapse."

Imam Ibn Taymiyyah Majmu' al-Fatawa (13th century)

Key Insight: Justice, not religious uniformity, is the foundation of social order.

Part V: Inner Peace (Sakina) and Spiritual Tranquility

13. Inner Peace Through Remembrance of Allah

Quran 13:28

**Those who have believed and whose hearts are assured by the remembrance of Allah. Unquestionably, by the remembrance of Allah hearts are assured.**

Key Term: Taṭmaʾinna (تطمئن) = To be at peace, tranquil, calm, assured

Islamic Psychology: True inner peace (sakina, tumaʾnīna) comes from:

  1. Connection with Allah: Through prayer (salah), remembrance (dhikr), recitation (tilawah)
  2. Trust in Allah: (Tawakkul) — surrendering anxiety about outcomes
  3. Contentment: (Ridā) — accepting Allah’s decree with peace
  4. Gratitude: (Shukr) — focusing on blessings, not deprivation

Sufi Perspective:

"The heart that is attached to the world is in constant turmoil—seeking what it doesn't have, fearing loss of what it has. But the heart attached to Allah—**the Eternal, the Unchanging**—rests in unshakable peace."

Imam Al-Ghazali Revival of the Religious Sciences (*Ihya Ulum al-Din*)

Contemporary Application: Islamic spiritual practices for mental health:

  • Salah (5 daily prayers): Mindfulness breaks, reduces anxiety
  • Dhikr (Remembrance): “Allah is sufficient for me” (Hasbiyallah)—reduces stress
  • Dua (Supplication): Spiritual equivalent of therapeutic journaling
  • Sabr (Patience): Resilience training in face of hardship

14. Submission (Islam) as Path to Peace

The word Islam itself means “peace through submission”—paradoxically, surrendering control brings peace.

Psychological Insight: Much human anxiety comes from:

  • Trying to control the uncontrollable (future, others’ actions, death)
  • Perfectionism and fear of failure
  • Attachment to specific outcomes

Islamic Solution:

  1. Do your best: Take action (sabab)
  2. Trust Allah: For the outcome (tawakkul)
  3. Accept the decree: Whatever happens was meant to be (qadar)

Prophetic Wisdom:

“Amazing is the affair of the believer—verily, all of his affair is good. If something good befalls him, he is grateful (shukr), and that is good for him. And if something harmful befalls him, he is patient (sabr), and that is good for him.” — Sahih Muslim 2999

Result: The believer is at peace in all circumstances—gratitude in ease, patience in hardship.

Part VI: Peace in Islamic Law (Fiqh)

15. Conditions for Justified Warfare in Islam

Islam permits warfare only under strict conditions:

Quran 2:190

**Fight in the way of Allah those who fight you but do not transgress. Indeed, Allah does not like transgressors.**

Classical Jurisprudence (Fiqh) on Just War:

Conditions for Permissible Fighting (from Maliki, Shafi’i, Hanbali, Hanafi schools):

  1. Defensive only: Fighting is permitted to repel aggression, not to initiate it
  2. Protect religious freedom: When Muslims are prevented from practicing Islam
  3. No transgression: “Do not transgress”—no excessive force, no targeting civilians
  4. Last resort: All peaceful options must be exhausted first
  5. Proportional response: Retaliation must match the harm (Quran 2:194)

Prohibited in War (from Hadith and Fiqh):

  • Killing non-combatants (women, children, elderly, monks, priests)
  • Mutilating corpses
  • Destroying crops, livestock, or property beyond military necessity
  • Torture or cruelty to prisoners
  • Using fire as weapon (Prophet’s explicit prohibition)
  • Treachery (breaking treaties or truce agreements)

"I have been informed that the Prophet ﷺ, whenever he sent out an army, would say: '**Do not kill children. Do not kill women. Do not kill the elderly. Do not cut down fruit-bearing trees. And do not destroy inhabited places.**'"

Imam Malik ibn Anas Al-Muwatta (8th century)

Modern Application: Many contemporary Muslim scholars (e.g., Sheikh Yusuf al-Qaradawi, Dr. Sherman Jackson) argue that terrorism violates all Islamic principles of warfare:

  • Targets civilians deliberately
  • Breaks covenants (visas, citizenship imply mutual security contracts)
  • Causes corruption (fasad) rather than establishing justice
  • Harms Islam’s reputation, hindering the da’wah (call to Islam)

16. The Ethics of Peace Treaties

Quranic Command:

Quran 16:91

**And fulfill the covenant of Allah when you have taken it [a pledge].**

Islamic Law on Treaties:

  1. Binding obligation: Breaking treaties is among the gravest sins
  2. No unilateral breaking: Cannot break treaty even if disadvantageous—unless other party violates first
  3. Transparency required: If you suspect betrayal, announce termination openly (Quran 8:58)—no surprise attacks
  4. Treaty with non-Muslims valid: Islamic law recognizes treaties with any state/group, regardless of religion

Prophetic Example: The Treaty of Hudaybiyyah—despite harsh terms and companion objections, Prophet Muhammad ﷺ honored it for two years until Quraysh violated it.

18. Countering Extremism with Islamic Peace Theology

Challenge: Extremist groups like Al-Qaeda, ISIS distort Islamic texts to justify violence.

Response: Mainstream Islamic scholars worldwide have issued fatwas (religious rulings) condemning terrorism and extremism:

1. Amman Message (2005)

  • Signed by 200+ Islamic scholars from 50 countries
  • Condemned terrorism and takfir (excommunicating other Muslims)
  • Affirmed diversity within Islamic jurisprudence

2. Marrakesh Declaration (2016)

  • 250+ Muslim scholars in Morocco
  • Reaffirmed Constitution of Medina as model for pluralistic societies
  • Called for full citizenship rights for religious minorities in Muslim-majority countries

3. Fatwa on Terrorism (Dr. Muhammad Tahir-ul-Qadri, 2010)

  • 600-page Islamic legal ruling declaring terrorism and suicide bombing absolutely prohibited (haram)
  • No exceptions—even for “defensive” claims
  • Widely distributed to counter radicalization

Key Theological Arguments Against Extremism:

  1. Killing innocents is murder: Quran 5:32 — “Whoever kills a soul…it is as if he had slain mankind entirely”
  2. Suicide is forbidden: Hadith explicitly prohibits self-killing under any circumstance
  3. Breaking treaties is grave sin: Modern citizenship/visas are treaties—attacking your host country violates covenant
  4. Fasad (corruption) prohibited: Terrorism creates chaos (fasad), the opposite of Islam’s goal (peace, justice)
  5. No vigilante justice: Only legitimate authorities can declare war—individual Muslims cannot take law into own hands

"**Extremism is not Islam gone too far—it is Islam gone astray.** The extremists have abandoned the scholarly tradition, ignored 1,400 years of jurisprudence, and substituted ignorance for knowledge."

Sheikh Abdullah bin Bayyah Forum for Promoting Peace in Muslim Societies

Part VIII: Practical Applications for Modern Muslims

19. Daily Practices to Embody Islamic Peace

1. Spread Salaam Universally

  • Greet everyone with “As-salamu alaykum”—not just Muslims
  • Smile when greeting (the Prophet ﷺ called smiling sadaqah)
  • Make eye contact—acknowledge the humanity of every person

2. Perform Salat al-Hajah (Prayer of Need) for World Peace

  • Two rak’ahs followed by dua for peace in conflict zones
  • Pray specifically for: Gaza, Syria, Yemen, Ukraine, Myanmar, etc.
  • “O Allah, You are As-Salaam, and from You comes all peace. Grant peace to the world.”

3. Practice “Repel Evil with Good” Daily

  • When someone is rude to you, respond with courtesy
  • When someone gossips, respond with praise of the person
  • When someone angers you, respond with patience (sabr)

4. Reconcile Broken Relationships

  • The Prophet ﷺ said not speaking to a Muslim beyond 3 days is sinful (Sahih Bukhari)
  • Initiate reconciliation even if you’re not “at fault”—peacemaking is more important than ego
  • Family reconciliation (silat al-rahim) is especially emphasized

5. Support Peace Organizations Financially

  • Give Zakat/Sadaqah to organizations mediating conflicts
  • Examples: KARAMAH (Muslim women’s rights), Islamic Relief (humanitarian aid in war zones)

6. Educate Yourself on Conflict Resolution

  • Learn Islamic mediation (sulh) principles
  • Study nonviolent communication (NVC) methods
  • Volunteer as mediator in community disputes

7. Challenge Extremist Narratives

  • When you encounter extremist rhetoric online, respond with authentic Islamic scholarship
  • Share articles by mainstream scholars refuting extremism
  • Report extremist content to platforms

8. Build Interfaith Relationships

  • Attend (or host) interfaith dialogues in your community
  • Visit churches, synagogues, temples during open houses
  • Invite neighbors of other faiths to your home for iftar (breaking fast)

9. Advocate for Justice Nonviolently

  • Participate in peaceful protests against oppression
  • Write to elected officials about unjust policies
  • Use social media to raise awareness—but always with wisdom and respect

10. Cultivate Inner Peace (Sakina)

  • Dhikr after each prayer: Subhanallah (33x), Alhamdulillah (33x), Allahu Akbar (34x)
  • Nighttime dua: “O Allah, I have submitted myself to You, entrusted my affairs to You…grant me peace”
  • Read Surah Al-Mulk (67) before sleep for tranquility

Part IX: Interfaith Comparison — Peace Across Traditions

Tradition Core Peace Teaching Divine Peace Concept Greeting of Peace
**Islam** "If they incline to peace, incline to it" (Quran 8:61) Allah is **As-Salaam** (The Peace)—peace is divine attribute ***As-salamu alaykum*** (Peace be upon you)
**Christianity** "Blessed are the peacemakers" (Matthew 5:9) God is God of Peace (Romans 15:33, Philippians 4:9) "Peace be with you" (Jesus's greeting post-resurrection)
**Judaism** "Seek peace and pursue it" (Psalm 34:14) **Shalom** (שָׁלוֹם) — wholeness, completeness, welfare ***Shalom aleichem*** (Peace be upon you)
**Buddhism** "Hatred never ceases by hatred, but by love alone" (Dhammapada 1:5) **Nirvana** — ultimate peace through cessation of craving "May all beings be happy and peaceful"

Part X: Addressing Difficult Questions

20. How Do Muslims Reconcile “Peace Religion” with Historical Violence?

Honest Acknowledgment: Like all major civilizations, Islamic history includes both exemplary moments of tolerance and periods of violence, conquest, and oppression. Claiming Islam has been consistently peaceful would be historically dishonest.

Key Distinctions:

1. Teachings vs. Practice

  • Teachings: The Quran and Hadith provide clear principles (peace as default, war as defensive, justice required, mercy emphasized)
  • Practice: Muslims—like followers of all religions—have often fallen short of their ideals
  • Analogy: Christianity teaches “love your enemies,” yet Christian history includes Crusades, Inquisition, colonialism—this doesn’t invalidate Jesus’s teachings

2. Context Matters

  • Medieval warfare norms: 7th-century Islamic conquests were comparatively restrained by contemporary standards (no total war, protection of non-combatants, religious tolerance)
  • Comparison: Muslim rule of Jerusalem (638-1099 CE) allowed Christian and Jewish worship; Crusader conquest (1099) massacred Muslims and Jews
  • Not justification: Just historical context—by modern standards, aggressive conquest is unacceptable regardless of era

3. Diversity of Islamic Practice

  • “Islam” is not monolithic: 1.8 billion Muslims across 200+ countries with vast interpretations
  • Peaceful majority: Vast majority of Muslims live peacefully; extremists are fringe (but loud) minority
  • Reform movements: Throughout history, Muslims have launched reform movements returning to Quran’s peace ethics (e.g., Abduh, Qutb’s early work, contemporary progressives)

Modern Muslim Consensus:

  • Aggressive warfare is not permissible (only defensive)
  • Forced conversion is absolutely prohibited
  • Terrorism is categorically forbidden (fatwas from all major scholarly bodies)
  • Pluralistic democracies are compatible with Islam (Marrakesh Declaration 2016)

"The real battle today is not Islam vs. the West, but **moderate, humanistic Islam vs. puritanical, extremist distortions of Islam**. The moderates must reclaim the narrative and demonstrate that peace, justice, and pluralism are Islam's authentic voice."

Dr. Khaled Abou El Fadl The Great Theft: Wrestling Islam from the Extremists (2005)

21. What About the “Sword Verse”? (Quran 9:5)

The Verse:

“And when the sacred months have passed, then kill the polytheists wherever you find them and capture them and besiege them and sit in wait for them at every place of ambush. But if they should repent, establish prayer, and give zakah, let them [go] on their way.” — Quran 9:5

Extremist Misuse: This verse is frequently cited by both anti-Muslim polemicists and extremist groups to claim Islam commands killing non-Muslims.

Classical Scholarly Consensus on Context (Tafsir):

1. Historical Context (Asbab al-Nuzul — Occasion of Revelation):

  • Specific to 9th year after Hijrah (631 CE)
  • Addresses specific pagan Arab tribes who repeatedly broke peace treaties with Muslims
  • Not universal command: Targets only those who violated treaties and actively waged war

2. Quranic Context (Read surrounding verses):

  • Verse 9:4: “Except those with whom you have treaty among the polytheists who have not been deficient [in fulfilling it]…fulfill their treaty” — Treaty-keepers are protected
  • Verse 9:6: “And if any one of the polytheists seeks your protection, then grant him protection so that he may hear the words of Allah. Then deliver him to his place of safety.” — Even enemies must be protected if they seek refuge
  • Verse 9:7: “So as long as they are upright toward you, be upright toward them” — Reciprocal peace

3. Legal Context (Abrogation Claims Rejected):

  • Claim: Some extremists say 9:5 “abrogates” (cancels) all peaceful verses
  • Scholarly Rejection: Classical scholars (Ibn Taymiyyah, Al-Qurtubi, Ibn Kathir) unanimously reject this—9:5 is specific command for specific situation, not universal principle
  • Proof: The Quran itself after 9:5 commands:
    • “If they incline to peace, incline to it” (8:61) — remains valid
    • “No compulsion in religion” (2:256) — remains valid

"Those who quote Surah 9:5 in isolation to claim Islam commands killing all non-Muslims are either **ignorant** or **deliberately deceitful**. The verse applies to specific treaty-breaking tribes in a specific historical moment, not to all non-Muslims for all time."

Sheikh Yusuf al-Qaradawi Fiqh of Jihad (2009)

Proper Understanding:

  • Specific, not general: Applies to active combatants who broke treaties
  • Defensive context: Muslims were fighting for survival against repeated attacks
  • Conditions apply: Offer peace first (9:6), honor treaties (9:4), accept peace if offered (8:61)
  • Classical consensus: All four Sunni schools + Shia scholars agree on contextual reading

Conclusion: The Vision of Dar al-Islam as Dar as-Salaam

The ultimate Islamic vision is to transform Dar al-Islam (The Abode of Islam) into Dar as-Salaam (The Abode of Peace)—a society characterized by:

  1. Justice for all: Muslim, non-Muslim, rich, poor—all receive fairness
  2. Inner peace: Hearts at rest through connection with As-Salaam (Allah)
  3. Social harmony: Reconciliation, forgiveness, compassion in relationships
  4. Economic equity: Wealth circulates, poverty addressed, exploitation prohibited
  5. Religious freedom: “No compulsion in religion”—all worship freely
  6. Environmental stewardship: Peace with creation (khalifa responsibility)
  7. Global cooperation: Muslims as “witnesses to humanity” (Quran 2:143)—advocating for peace and justice worldwide

  • His character was gentleness even toward enemies

  • His treaties prioritized long-term peace over short-term gains
  • His final sermon emphasized sanctity of life, property, and honor

  • In worship (surrender to Allah)
  • In character (mercy, gentleness, patience)
  • In relationships (forgiveness, reconciliation)
  • In society (justice, equity, compassion)
  • In the world (peacebuilding, conflict resolution)

True strength is self-mastery. True Islam is peace—inner and outer, personal and political, spiritual and social.
