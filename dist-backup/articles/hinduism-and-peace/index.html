Part I: Ahimsa (Non-Violence) ‚Äî Hinduism‚Äôs Supreme Ethic

1. Ahimsa Defined

Ahimsa (‡§Ö‡§π‡§ø‡§Ç‡§∏‡§æ):

  • A- (‡§Ö) = Not, without
  • Himsa (‡§π‡§ø‡§Ç‡§∏‡§æ) = Violence, harm, injury

Literal meaning: Non-violence, non-harming, absence of desire to injure

Scope:

  • Not just physical violence: Includes mental (thoughts of hatred), verbal (hurtful speech), and physical harm
  • Positive virtue: Not merely abstaining from violence but actively cultivating compassion
  • Universal application: Toward humans, animals, plants, environment‚Äîall life is sacred

**Non-violence is the highest ethical principle.**

3. Mahatma Gandhi ‚Äî Ahimsa as Political Revolution

Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi (1869-1948): The 20th century‚Äôs greatest exponent of ahimsa

Gandhi‚Äôs Definition:

‚ÄúAhimsa is not merely a negative state of harmlessness but a positive state of love, of doing good even to the evildoer. Ahimsa is the basis of the search for truth.‚Äù

  • Meaning: Holding firmly to truth through nonviolent resistance

  • Civil disobedience: Break unjust laws openly, accept punishment
  • Suffering love: Endure violence without retaliation‚Äî‚Äúturn the other cheek‚Äù
  • Appeal to oppressor‚Äôs conscience: Demonstrate moral superiority of nonviolence

2. Ahimsa in Thought, Word, Deed:

  • Verbal ahimsa: No insults, lies, harsh speech

  • Self-purification: Fast, prayer, manual labor (spinning khadi)‚Äîtransform yourself first

  • Inspired global movements: Martin Luther King Jr., Nelson Mandela, Cesar Chavez, Aung San Suu Kyi

Part III: The Bhagavad Gita ‚Äî Peace Amidst Conflict

6. Context: Arjuna‚Äôs Crisis

The Bhagavad Gita (‡§≠‡§ó‡§µ‡§¶‡•ç‡§ó‡•Ä‡§§‡§æ, ‚ÄúSong of God‚Äù) is a 700-verse dialogue between Prince Arjuna and Lord Krishna (incarnation of God) on the battlefield of Kurukshetra.

Setup:

  • Arjuna (warrior prince) faces his relatives, teachers, friends in battle
  • Moral dilemma: Dharma (duty as warrior) requires fighting, but ahimsa forbids killing loved ones
  • Arjuna‚Äôs breakdown: ‚ÄúI will not fight‚Äù (refuses to participate, drops bow)

Krishna‚Äôs Response: Not ‚Äúfight‚Äù or ‚Äúdon‚Äôt fight,‚Äù but transcend the dilemma by understanding:

  1. Dharma (righteous duty)
  2. Karma Yoga (selfless action)
  3. Atman (the indestructible soul)

7. Karma Yoga ‚Äî The Path of Selfless Action

Bhagavad Gita 2.47

**You have a right to perform your prescribed duty, but you are not entitled to the fruits of action. Never consider yourself the cause of the results of your activities, and never be attached to not doing your duty.**

Key Principle: Do your duty, but renounce attachment to results.

How This Creates Peace:

  1. Eliminates anxiety: If you‚Äôre not attached to outcomes, failure doesn‚Äôt devastate you
  2. Stops ego: ‚ÄúI am the doer‚Äù creates pride (success) or shame (failure)‚Äîboth disturb peace
  3. Purifies motive: Work becomes service, not self-aggrandizement
  4. Reduces conflict: When you don‚Äôt crave specific outcomes, fewer clashes with others‚Äô desires

Example:

  • Attached: ‚ÄúI must win this election or my life is ruined‚Äù ‚Üí constant stress, willing to cheat
  • Detached: ‚ÄúI‚Äôll campaign honestly; if I win, I serve; if I lose, I tried my best‚Äù ‚Üí inner peace regardless

Bhagavad Gita 2.48

**Perform your duty equipoised, O Arjuna, abandoning all attachment to success or failure. Such equanimity is called yoga.**

Samatva (‡§∏‡§Æ‡§§‡•ç‡§µ) = Equanimity, evenness, balance‚Äîthe hallmark of inner peace.

8. The Indestructible Atman

Krishna‚Äôs Teaching: You are not the body; you are the Atman (‡§Ü‡§§‡•ç‡§Æ‡§®‡•ç)‚Äîthe eternal soul.

Bhagavad Gita 2.20

**The soul is never born, nor does it ever die. Having come into being once, it never ceases to be. It is unborn, eternal, ever-existing, and primeval. It is not slain when the body is slain.**

Implication: Death is not ultimate tragedy‚Äîit‚Äôs shedding old clothes (body) for new. Therefore:

  • Fear of death diminishes: Peace comes from knowing your essence is indestructible
  • Violence becomes absurd: You can‚Äôt kill the soul; harming the body is like tearing someone‚Äôs coat

Paradox: The Gita uses war as metaphor for inner struggle‚Äîfighting ego, desire, ignorance. Physical war becomes symbol of spiritual battle.

9. Seeing the Divine in All Beings

Bhagavad Gita 6.29

**A true yogi observes Me (God) in all beings, and also sees every being in Me. Indeed, the self-realized person sees Me, the same Supreme Lord, everywhere.**

When you see God in all:

  • Harming another is harming God
  • Serving another is serving God
  • Conflicts dissolve‚Äîall are manifestations of one reality

This is the philosophical foundation of ahimsa: Not just ethical rule, but ontological truth‚Äîwe are one.

12. Meditation (Dhyana) Practice

Duration: 15-30 minutes Frequency: Daily, ideally at sunrise Posture: Seated cross-legged (Padmasana), spine straight, hands in Gyana Mudra (thumb-index finger touching)

Instructions:

Part V: Dharma ‚Äî Cosmic Order and Social Peace

13. Dharma Defined

Dharma (‡§ß‡§∞‡•ç‡§Æ):

  • Root: dhr (‡§ß‡•É) = To uphold, support, sustain
  • Meanings: Duty, righteousness, cosmic law, ethical order, religion

Four Dimensions of Dharma:

  1. Sanatana Dharma (‡§∏‡§®‡§æ‡§§‡§® ‡§ß‡§∞‡•ç‡§Æ): Eternal, universal principles (truth, non-violence, compassion)
  2. Varna Dharma (‡§µ‡§∞‡•ç‡§£ ‡§ß‡§∞‡•ç‡§Æ): Duties according to social class (historically: Brahmin, Kshatriya, Vaishya, Shudra)‚ÄîNote: Modern Hindus increasingly reject caste as oppressive, emphasizing universal dharma
  3. Ashrama Dharma (‡§Ü‡§∂‡•ç‡§∞‡§Æ ‡§ß‡§∞‡•ç‡§Æ): Duties according to life stage (student, householder, retiree, renunciant)
  4. Svadharma (‡§∏‡•ç‡§µ‡§ß‡§∞‡•ç‡§Æ): Personal duty, unique purpose

Why Dharma Creates Peace:

  • When everyone fulfills dharma: Society functions harmoniously (like organs in body)
  • When dharma is violated: Chaos, injustice, conflict (adharma)
  • Personal peace: Comes from living authentically according to your unique purpose

Bhagavad Gita 4.7

**Whenever there is a decline in dharma and a rise in adharma, O Bharata, at that time I manifest Myself.**

Meaning: God (Krishna) incarnates to restore dharma when evil threatens cosmic order. Hindus believe this has happened 9 times (Rama, Krishna, etc.); 10th incarnation (Kalki) yet to come.

16. Living Ahimsa Daily

1. Diet:

  • Vegetarianism: Traditional Hindu practice (not all Hindus are vegetarian, but many are)
  • Why: Ahimsa toward animals‚Äîavoid causing suffering through food choices
  • Modern: Even one meatless day per week (Meatless Monday) honors ahimsa
