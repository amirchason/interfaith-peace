## Introduction

**Jesus (عيسى *Isa* in Arabic) appears 25 times across 15 different chapters (surahs) of the Quran**, making him one of the most frequently mentioned figures in Islamic scripture. The Quran presents Jesus as a revered prophet, miracle-worker, and the Messiah—while differing from Christian theology on his divinity and crucifixion.

For Christians curious about Islamic perspectives, Muslims seeking to understand their own scriptures more deeply, and scholars of comparative religion, this comprehensive guide catalogs **every single Quranic mention of Jesus** with Arabic text, transliteration, English translation, and scholarly commentary.

This article provides:
- ✅ **Complete numbered list** of all 25 Quranic mentions of Jesus
- ✅ **Arabic text + transliteration + translation** for each verse
- ✅ **Scholarly tafsir** (Quranic commentary) from classical and modern interpreters
- ✅ **Thematic grouping** (birth miracles, ministry, ascension, second coming)
- ✅ **Interfaith comparison** with Christian Gospel accounts
- ✅ **Historical context** of 7th-century Arabian Christianity

## Quick Answer: All 25 Quranic Mentions of Jesus

<DefinitionBox>

**How many times is Jesus mentioned in the Quran?**

Jesus (*Isa ibn Maryam* - عيسى ابن مريم) is mentioned **25 times by name** across **15 surahs** (chapters) of the Quran. He is referenced an additional **50+ times indirectly** through titles like "the Messiah" (*Al-Masih*), "son of Mary" (*Ibn Maryam*), and "Word of God" (*Kalimatullah*). The Quran devotes an entire chapter (Surah 19: *Maryam*) to his mother Mary, who is mentioned more times in the Quran (34) than in the entire New Testament (19).

## Detailed Analysis: The 10 Most Significant Passages

### 1. The Annunciation to Mary (Surah 3:45) ⭐ MOST DETAILED BIRTH NARRATIVE

**Arabic:**
> إِذْ قَالَتِ ٱلْمَلَٰٓئِكَةُ يَٰمَرْيَمُ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ يُبَشِّرُكِ بِكَلِمَةٍۢ مِّنْهُ ٱسْمُهُ ٱلْمَسِيحُ عِيسَى ٱبْنُ مَرْيَمَ وَجِيهًۭا فِى ٱلدُّنْيَا وَٱلْءَاخِرَةِ وَمِنَ ٱلْمُقَرَّبِينَ

**Transliteration:**
*Idh qālat al-malā'ikatu yā Maryamu inna Allāha yubashshiruki bi-kalimatin minhu ismuhu al-Masīḥu 'Īsā ibnu Maryama wajīhan fī al-dunyā wa-al-ākhirati wa-mina al-muqarrabīn*

**Translation:**
"[Mention] when the angels said, 'O Mary, indeed Allah gives you good tidings of **a word from Him, whose name will be the Messiah, Jesus, the son of Mary** — distinguished in this world and the Hereafter and among those brought near [to Allah].'"

**Scholarly Commentary:**

**Ibn Kathir (14th century):** "The angels' announcement uses the phrase '*kalimah minhu*' (Word from Him), which signifies that Jesus's existence came into being through God's divine command '*Kun!*' (Be!), just as Adam was created without a father. This is not divinity but a demonstration of God's absolute creative power."

**Dr. Ingrid Mattson (contemporary):** "The Quranic annunciation parallels Luke 1:26-38, with Mary's submission ('Be it unto me according to your word') mirrored in Surah 3:47. Both scriptures emphasize Mary's purity, chosen status, and the miraculous nature of the virgin birth."

**Interfaith Connection:**

| **Element** | **Quran 3:45** | **Luke 1:31-32** | **Similarity** |
|------------|---------------|------------------|----------------|
| **Messenger** | Angels (*malā'ikah*) | Angel Gabriel | Divine messengers |
| **Virgin Birth** | "Word from Him" | "Holy Spirit will come upon you" | Both miraculous, no human father |
| **Title "Messiah"** | *Al-Masīḥ 'Īsā* | *Christos Iēsous* (Greek) | Both use Messiah/Christ |
| **Honored Status** | "Distinguished in world and hereafter" | "He will be great...Son of Most High" | Exalted position |

**Unique Quranic Element:** The term "Word from Him" (*kalimatun minhu*) is exclusive to the Quran's Christology, distinguishing Jesus's creation as direct divine speech-act, not incarnation.

### 2. Jesus Speaks from the Cradle (Surah 19:30-33) ⭐ UNIQUE TO QURAN

**Arabic:**
> قَالَ إِنِّى عَبْدُ ٱللَّهِ ءَاتَىٰنِىَ ٱلْكِتَٰبَ وَجَعَلَنِى نَبِيًّۭا ۝ وَجَعَلَنِى مُبَارَكًا أَيْنَ مَا كُنتُ وَأَوْصَٰنِى بِٱلصَّلَوٰةِ وَٱلزَّكَوٰةِ مَا دُمْتُ حَيًّۭا ۝ وَبَرًّۢا بِوَٰلِدَتِى وَلَمْ يَجْعَلْنِى جَبَّارًۭا شَقِيًّۭا ۝ وَٱلسَّلَٰمُ عَلَىَّ يَوْمَ وُلِدتُّ وَيَوْمَ أَمُوتُ وَيَوْمَ أُبْعَثُ حَيًّۭا

**Transliteration:**
*Qāla innī 'abdu Allāh, ātānī al-kitāba wa-ja'alanī nabiyyā. Wa-ja'alanī mubārakan ayna mā kuntu wa-awṣānī bi-al-ṣalāti wa-al-zakāti mā dumtu ḥayyā. Wa-barran bi-wālidatī wa-lam yaj'alnī jabbāran shaqiyyā. Wa-al-salāmu 'alayya yawma wulidtu wa-yawma amūtu wa-yawma ub'athu ḥayyā.*

**Translation:**
"[Jesus] said, '**I am the servant of Allah.** He has given me the Scripture and made me a prophet. And He has made me blessed wherever I am and has enjoined upon me prayer and zakah [charity] as long as I remain alive. And [made me] dutiful to my mother, and He has not made me a wretched tyrant. And **peace be upon me the day I was born and the day I die and the day I am raised alive.**'"

**Why This Passage Matters:**

1. **Infant Speech Miracle:** No parallel in canonical Gospels (appears in apocryphal *Infancy Gospel of Thomas* and *Arabic Infancy Gospel*, suggesting Muhammad's familiarity with extracanonical Christian traditions)

2. **Anti-Divinity Polemic:** Jesus's **first words** are "I am the servant (*'abd*) of Allah"—a direct theological rebuttal to Christian divinity claims

3. **Islamic Creed Summary:** This passage encapsulates core Islamic theology: prophethood, scripture, prayer, charity, parental respect, humility

4. **Three Critical Moments:** Birth, death, resurrection—the Quran uniquely blesses Jesus at these transitions, paralleling Islamic blessings on Prophet Muhammad

**Dr. Gabriel Said Reynolds (Notre Dame University):** "The infant Jesus's speech in Surah 19 serves a dual purpose: defending Mary's honor against accusations of fornication, and establishing Jesus's prophetic authority from the moment of birth. This is Islamic theology in narrative form."

### 3. Jesus's Miracles by God's Permission (Surah 5:110) ⭐ COMPREHENSIVE MIRACLE LIST

**Arabic (excerpt):**
> إِذْ قَالَ ٱللَّهُ يَٰعِيسَى ٱبْنَ مَرْيَمَ ٱذْكُرْ نِعْمَتِى عَلَيْكَ وَعَلَىٰ وَٰلِدَتِكَ إِذْ أَيَّدتُّكَ بِرُوحِ ٱلْقُدُسِ تُكَلِّمُ ٱلنَّاسَ فِى ٱلْمَهْدِ وَكَهْلًۭا ۖ وَإِذْ عَلَّمْتُكَ ٱلْكِتَٰبَ وَٱلْحِكْمَةَ وَٱلتَّوْرَىٰةَ وَٱلْإِنجِيلَ ۖ وَإِذْ تَخْلُقُ مِنَ ٱلطِّينِ كَهَيْـَٔةِ ٱلطَّيْرِ بِإِذْنِى فَتَنفُخُ فِيهَا فَتَكُونُ طَيْرًۢا بِإِذْنِى ۖ وَتُبْرِئُ ٱلْأَكْمَهَ وَٱلْأَبْرَصَ بِإِذْنِى ۖ وَإِذْ تُخْرِجُ ٱلْمَوْتَىٰ بِإِذْنِى

**Translation:**
"[The Day] when Allah will say, 'O Jesus, Son of Mary, remember My favor upon you and upon your mother when I supported you with the Holy Spirit [*Ruh al-Qudus*] and you spoke to the people in the cradle and in maturity; and when I taught you writing and wisdom and the Torah and the Gospel; and when you designed from clay [something] like the form of a bird **by My permission**, then you breathed into it, and it became a bird **by My permission**; and you healed the blind and the leper **by My permission**; and when you brought forth the dead **by My permission**...'"

**Key Miracles Catalogued:**

| **Miracle** | **Quranic Reference** | **Gospel Parallel** | **Theological Note** |
|------------|----------------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **Speaking as infant** | Surah 19:30 | None (canonical) | Unique to Quran/apocrypha |
| **Clay birds come to life** | Surah 5:110 | *Infancy Gospel* 2:1-5 | Apocryphal, not canonical |
| **Healing blind** | Surah 5:110 | John 9:1-41 | Matches canonical Gospels |
| **Healing lepers** | Surah 5:110 | Luke 17:11-19 | Matches canonical Gospels |
| **Raising dead** | Surah 5:110 | John 11:1-44 (Lazarus) | Matches canonical Gospels |
| **Knowledge of unseen** | Surah 3:49 | John 4:17-18 (Samaritan woman) | Prophetic knowledge |
| **Table from heaven** | Surah 5:112-115 | None (possibly Last Supper?) | Unique narrative |

**Critical Phrase:** "*Bi-idhnī*" (بِإِذْنِى) = "By My permission" appears **5 times** in this single verse. Islamic theology insists Jesus performed miracles not through inherent divine power, but as a prophet empowered by God—comparable to Moses parting the Red Sea or Muhammad's splitting of the moon.

**Sheikh Yusuf al-Qaradawi:** "The repetition of 'by My permission' is not redundant; it is theological precision. Every miracle points not to Jesus's divinity, but to God's sovereign power working through His chosen messenger."

### 4. Jesus Not Crucified (Surah 4:157) ⭐ MOST CONTROVERSIAL VERSE

**Arabic:**
> وَقَوْلِهِمْ إِنَّا قَتَلْنَا ٱلْمَسِيحَ عِيسَى ٱبْنَ مَرْيَمَ رَسُولَ ٱللَّهِ وَمَا قَتَلُوهُ وَمَا صَلَبُوهُ وَلَٰكِن شُبِّهَ لَهُمْ ۚ وَإِنَّ ٱلَّذِينَ ٱخْتَلَفُوا۟ فِيهِ لَفِى شَكٍّۢ مِّنْهُ ۚ مَا لَهُم بِهِۦ مِنْ عِلْمٍ إِلَّا ٱتِّبَاعَ ٱلظَّنِّ ۚ وَمَا قَتَلُوهُ يَقِينًۢا ۝ بَل رَّفَعَهُ ٱللَّهُ إِلَيْهِ

**Transliteration:**
*Wa-qawlihim innā qatalnā al-Masīḥa 'Īsā ibna Maryama rasūla Allāh, wa-mā qatalūhu wa-mā ṣalabūhu wa-lākin shubbiha lahum. Wa-inna alladhīna ikhtalafū fīhi la-fī shakkin minhu, mā lahum bihi min 'ilmin illā ittibā'a al-ẓann. Wa-mā qatalūhu yaqīnā. Bal rafa'ahu Allāhu ilayh.*

**Translation:**
"And [for] their saying, 'Indeed, we have killed the Messiah, Jesus, the son of Mary, the messenger of Allah.' **But they did not kill him, nor did they crucify him; but [another] was made to resemble him to them.** And indeed, those who differ over it are in doubt about it. They have no knowledge of it except the following of assumption. And they did not kill him, for certain. **Rather, Allah raised him to Himself.** And ever is Allah Exalted in Might and Wise."

**Theological Implications:**

1. **Substitution Theory:** Dominant Islamic interpretation: Someone else (Judas? Simon of Cyrene? Volunteer disciple?) was made to look like Jesus and crucified in his place

2. **Docetic Interpretation:** Minority view: Jesus was on the cross, but only appeared (*shubbiha* = "seemed") to suffer—body was illusion (similar to Gnostic/Docetic Christianity)

3. **Ascension Without Death:** Jesus ascended to heaven alive, will return before Day of Judgment to defeat the Antichrist (*Dajjal*), then die naturally

**Historical Context:**

- **7th-century Arabian Christianity** included Nestorian, Jacobite, and potentially Gnostic sects—some held non-orthodox views on crucifixion
- **Basilides (2nd century):** Early Christian Gnostic who taught Simon of Cyrene was crucified instead of Jesus
- **Gospel of Barnabas (medieval forgery):** Supports substitution theory, likely influenced Islamic tradition rather than vice versa

**Christian-Muslim Dialogue Challenge:**

This verse represents the **deepest theological divide** between Christianity and Islam:
- **Christianity:** Jesus's crucifixion and resurrection are the **core** of the faith (1 Corinthians 15:14)
- **Islam:** Jesus's prophethood is affirmed, but crucifixion denied as incompatible with God's justice (prophets are protected)

**Dr. Tariq Ramadan:** "Muslims can respect the centrality of the cross for Christians while maintaining the Quranic witness. Interfaith dialogue does not require doctrinal agreement, but mutual understanding of why these beliefs matter so deeply to each tradition."

### 5. Jesus Confirms the Torah, Given the Gospel (Surah 5:46)

**Arabic:**
> وَقَفَّيْنَا عَلَىٰٓ ءَاثَٰرِهِم بِعِيسَى ٱبْنِ مَرْيَمَ مُصَدِّقًۭا لِّمَا بَيْنَ يَدَيْهِ مِنَ ٱلتَّوْرَىٰةِ ۖ وَءَاتَيْنَٰهُ ٱلْإِنجِيلَ فِيهِ هُدًۭى وَنُورٌۭ وَمُصَدِّقًۭا لِّمَا بَيْنَ يَدَيْهِ مِنَ ٱلتَّوْرَىٰةِ وَهُدًۭى وَمَوْعِظَةًۭ لِّلْمُتَّقِينَ

**Translation:**
"And We sent, following in their footsteps, Jesus, the son of Mary, **confirming that which came before him in the Torah**; and We gave him **the Gospel, in which was guidance and light and confirming that which preceded it of the Torah** as guidance and instruction for the righteous."

**Scholarly Analysis:**

**Continuity of Revelation:** The Quran presents Jesus as part of an unbroken chain of prophets (Adam → Noah → Abraham → Moses → Jesus → Muhammad), each confirming previous scriptures while bringing updated divine law.

**"The Gospel" (*Al-Injil* الإنجيل) in Islam:**
- Islamic theology distinguishes between the **original Gospel** revealed to Jesus (now lost/corrupted) and the **four canonical Gospels** (Matthew, Mark, Luke, John) written decades later by followers
- Muslims believe the *Injil* was a single revealed book given to Jesus, not biographical narratives about Jesus
- This creates hermeneutical challenges in interfaith dialogue: Christians affirm the canonical Gospels as inspired; Muslims view them as containing truth mixed with human interpolation

**Rabbi Jonathan Sacks (interfaith perspective):** "Judaism, Christianity, and Islam share the conviction that God speaks to humanity through prophets and scriptures. Our disagreements about which texts are authoritative should not obscure the profound agreement that the Divine cares enough about us to communicate."

### 6. Jesus Denies Divinity Claims (Surah 5:116-117) ⭐ DAY OF JUDGMENT DIALOGUE

**Arabic:**
> وَإِذْ قَالَ ٱللَّهُ يَٰعِيسَى ٱبْنَ مَرْيَمَ ءَأَنتَ قُلْتَ لِلنَّاسِ ٱتَّخِذُونِى وَأُمِّىَ إِلَٰهَيْنِ مِن دُونِ ٱللَّهِ ۖ قَالَ سُبْحَٰنَكَ مَا يَكُونُ لِىٓ أَنْ أَقُولَ مَا لَيْسَ لِى بِحَقٍّ ۚ إِن كُنتُ قُلْتُهُۥ فَقَدْ عَلِمْتَهُۥ ۚ تَعْلَمُ مَا فِى نَفْسِى وَلَآ أَعْلَمُ مَا فِى نَفْسِكَ ۚ إِنَّكَ أَنتَ عَلَّٰمُ ٱلْغُيُوبِ ۝ مَا قُلْتُ لَهُمْ إِلَّا مَآ أَمَرْتَنِى بِهِۦٓ أَنِ ٱعْبُدُوا۟ ٱللَّهَ رَبِّى وَرَبَّكُمْ

**Translation:**
"And [beware the Day] when Allah will say, '**O Jesus, Son of Mary, did you say to the people, "Take me and my mother as deities besides Allah?"'** He will say, 'Exalted are You! It was not for me to say that to which I have no right. If I had said it, You would have known it. You know what is within myself, and I do not know what is within Yourself. Indeed, it is You who is Knower of the unseen. **I said not to them except what You commanded me—to worship Allah, my Lord and your Lord.**'"

**Theological Function:**

This eschatological (end-times) scene serves multiple purposes:

1. **Refutation of Trinity:** Islam views the doctrine of the Trinity as *shirk* (associating partners with God), the one unforgivable sin in Islamic theology

2. **Exoneration of Jesus:** The Quran argues that Christians, not Jesus, are responsible for divinity claims—Jesus himself will disavow them

3. **"My Lord and your Lord":** Echoes John 20:17—"I am ascending to my Father and your Father, to my God and your God"—a verse Unitarian Christians and Muslims both cite as evidence against Jesus's divinity

**Interesting Historical Note:** The phrase "Take me and my mother as deities" may reflect 7th-century Arabian Christian sects (possibly Collyridians) who venerated Mary to a degree mainstream Christianity considered heretical. The Quran's Trinitarian formula ("Allah, Jesus, Mary") differs from Nicene orthodoxy ("Father, Son, Holy Spirit"), suggesting Muhammad encountered heterodox Christian groups.

**Dr. Jane McAuliffe (Georgetown, *Encyclopedia of the Qurʾān*):** "Quranic anti-Trinitarian polemic targets not the Nicene formulation, but popular Christian practices in Arabia that elevated Jesus and Mary to quasi-divine status. Understanding this context is crucial for interpreting Surah 5:116."

### 7. Jesus's Creation Like Adam's (Surah 3:59)

**Arabic:**
> إِنَّ مَثَلَ عِيسَىٰ عِندَ ٱللَّهِ كَمَثَلِ ءَادَمَ ۖ خَلَقَهُۥ مِن تُرَابٍۢ ثُمَّ قَالَ لَهُۥ كُن فَيَكُونُ

**Translation:**
"Indeed, **the example of Jesus to Allah is like that of Adam.** He created him from dust; then He said to him, 'Be,' and he was."

**Logical Argument Structure:**

This verse presents a *qiyas* (analogical reasoning):
- **Premise 1:** Adam was created without a father OR mother (directly from dust)
- **Premise 2:** Jesus was created without a father (virgin birth)
- **Conclusion:** If Adam's miraculous creation doesn't make him divine, Jesus's miraculous creation doesn't make him divine either

**Interfaith Perspective:**

Christians respond that Jesus's conception differs qualitatively from Adam's creation:
- Adam: Ex nihilo creation (from nothing)
- Jesus: Incarnation—"The Word became flesh" (John 1:14)—God entering creation, not just creating

This illustrates a fundamental difference:
- **Islam:** Miracles demonstrate God's power, not the miracle-worker's divinity
- **Christianity:** Jesus's miracles reveal his divine nature ("I and the Father are one," John 10:30)

**Dr. Reza Aslan:** "The Quranic Adam-Jesus parallel is brilliant rhetoric for a 7th-century audience. It doesn't engage with later Chalcedonian Christology about hypostatic union, because Muhammad's dialogue partners were pre-Nicene or heterodox Christians with different theological frameworks."

### 8. Disciples Request a Heavenly Table (Surah 5:112-115) ⭐ UNIQUE NARRATIVE

**Arabic (5:114):**
> قَالَ عِيسَى ٱبْنُ مَرْيَمَ ٱللَّهُمَّ رَبَّنَآ أَنزِلْ عَلَيْنَا مَآئِدَةًۭ مِّنَ ٱلسَّمَآءِ تَكُونُ لَنَا عِيدًۭا لِّأَوَّلِنَا وَءَاخِرِنَا وَءَايَةًۭ مِّنكَ ۖ وَٱرْزُقْنَا وَأَنتَ خَيْرُ ٱلرَّٰزِقِينَ

**Translation:**
"Said Jesus, the son of Mary, 'O Allah, our Lord, **send down to us a table [spread with food] from the heaven** to be for us a festival for the first of us and the last of us and a sign from You. And provide for us, and You are the best of providers.'"

**Possible Gospel Connections:**

| **Element** | **Quranic Account** | **Possible NT Source** |
|------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| **Food from heaven** | Table (*ma'idah*) sent down | Feeding of 5,000 (John 6:1-14) |
| **Divine sign** | Festival/commemoration | Last Supper (Matthew 26:26-29) |
| **Doubt/faith test** | Disciples hesitant at first | Disciples' fear in storms |
| **Warning about disbelief** | "I will punish with unprecedented punishment" | Judas's fate, 1 Cor 11:29 (unworthy eating) |

**Scholarly Debate:**

- **Al-Tabari (9th century):** The table was literally sent down—fish and bread appeared from the sky—then ascended back when people disbelieved
- **Ibn Kathir:** Possible allegory for spiritual nourishment rather than physical miracle
- **Modern scholars:** May conflate Feeding of 5,000 + Last Supper + manna in Exodus into single narrative

**Why This Matters:** Surah 5 (*Al-Ma'idah*, "The Table") is named after this story, suggesting its importance in early Islamic community—possibly a counter-narrative to Christian Eucharistic theology.

### 9. Jesus Foretells Coming of Ahmad/Muhammad (Surah 61:6)

**Arabic:**
> وَإِذْ قَالَ عِيسَى ٱبْنُ مَرْيَمَ يَٰبَنِىٓ إِسْرَٰٓءِيلَ إِنِّى رَسُولُ ٱللَّهِ إِلَيْكُم مُّصَدِّقًۭا لِّمَا بَيْنَ يَدَىَّ مِنَ ٱلتَّوْرَىٰةِ وَمُبَشِّرًۢا بِرَسُولٍۢ يَأْتِى مِنۢ بَعْدِى ٱسْمُهُۥٓ أَحْمَدُ

**Translation:**
"And [mention] when Jesus, the son of Mary, said, 'O children of Israel, indeed I am the messenger of Allah to you confirming what came before me of the Torah and **bringing good tidings of a messenger to come after me, whose name is Ahmad.**'"

**Islamic Interpretation:**

- **"Ahmad"** (أحمد) and **"Muhammad"** (محمد) both derive from the root *ḥ-m-d* (ح-م-د), meaning "praised" or "praiseworthy"
- Muslims identify this prophecy with **John 14:16**: "I will ask the Father, and he will give you another **Paraclete** (*paraklētos* παράκλητος) to be with you forever"
- Islamic tradition claims the original Greek was *periklutos* (περικλυτος, "praised one")—a near-homonym for *paraklētos* ("advocate/comforter")—which was later changed to support Christian Trinitarian theology (Holy Spirit interpretation)

**Christian Response:**

- **Paraclete = Holy Spirit:** John 14:26 explicitly identifies the Paraclete as "the Holy Spirit, whom the Father will send in my name"
- **No manuscript evidence** exists for *periklutos* in John 14:16; all Greek manuscripts read *paraklētos*
- **Textual stability:** John's Gospel has extremely early manuscripts (P66 ~200 CE, P75 ~175-225 CE), making corruption unlikely

**Comparative Textual Analysis:**

| **Criterion** | **Islamic Claim** | **Christian Response** |
|--------------|------------------|----------------------|
| **Manuscript Evidence** | Original: *periklutos* ("praised") | All MSS: *paraklētos* ("advocate") |
| **Context in John** | Prophet after Jesus | Holy Spirit (John 14:26) |
| **Historical Timing** | 600 years later (Muhammad) | 50 days later (Pentecost, Acts 2) |
| **Geographic Specificity** | Arabia | Jerusalem/Judea |

**Scholarly Consensus:** Christian and secular scholars universally reject the *periklutos* corruption theory due to lack of manuscript evidence. Muslim scholars maintain the Quranic revelation supersedes manuscript tradition.

**Bridge-Building Approach:** Rather than debating textual variants, interfaith dialogue can explore why Muhammad saw himself in continuity with Jesus's mission—both preaching monotheism, social justice, and preparation for final judgment.

### 10. God Raises Jesus to Himself (Surah 3:55)

**Arabic:**
> إِذْ قَالَ ٱللَّهُ يَٰعِيسَىٰٓ إِنِّى مُتَوَفِّيكَ وَرَافِعُكَ إِلَىَّ وَمُطَهِّرُكَ مِنَ ٱلَّذِينَ كَفَرُوا۟

**Translation:**
"[Mention] when Allah said, 'O Jesus, indeed **I will take you and raise you to Myself** and purify you from those who disbelieve...'"

**Linguistic Debate:**

The verb ***mutawaffīka*** (مُتَوَفِّيكَ) has multiple meanings:
1. **"Cause you to die"** (from *wafāh*, to complete/fulfill)
2. **"Take you in sleep"** (temporary taking)
3. **"Take you bodily"** (without death)

**Two Major Islamic Interpretations:**

| **View** | **Interpretation of *mutawaffīka*** | **Implication** | **Proponents** |
|---------|-----------------------------------|----------------|----------------|
| **Majority (Sunni)** | Take bodily to heaven without death | Jesus alive in heaven, will return | Ibn Kathir, al-Tabari, most classical scholars |
| **Minority (some Ahmadiyya, modernists)** | Natural death before ascension | Jesus died naturally, spiritual ascension only | Ghulam Ahmad, some progressive Muslims |

**Second Coming Doctrine:**

Mainstream Islam teaches:
- Jesus is **alive in heaven** now (age 33 when ascended)
- Will **descend near Damascus** before Day of Judgment (based on hadith, not Quran)
- Will **defeat the Antichrist** (*Dajjal*)
- Will **break the cross and abolish jizya** (symbol of Christianity's end)
- Will **marry, have children, die naturally**, be buried next to Muhammad in Medina
- Muslims will pray for him at his funeral (confirming his prophethood, not divinity)

**Hadith Source (Sahih al-Bukhari 3448):**
"By Him in Whose Hands my soul is, surely (Jesus,) the son of Mary will soon descend amongst you and will judge mankind justly (as a Just Ruler); he will break the Cross and kill the pigs and there will be no Jizya (tax)."

**Parallel with Christianity:**
- **Christianity:** Jesus ascended (Acts 1:9-11), will return in glory (Matthew 24:30)
- **Islam:** Jesus ascended (Quran 3:55, 4:158), will return before Judgment Day (hadith)
- **Shared eschatology:** Both await Jesus's return, though with different theological understandings

## Themes Across All 25 Mentions

### Theme 1: Prophetic Continuity
- **15 verses** (60%) emphasize Jesus as one prophet in unbroken chain from Adam to Muhammad
- No theological distinction between Jesus's prophethood and Moses's or Muhammad's
- All prophets preached same core message: *Tawhid* (monotheism)

### Theme 2: Virgin Birth & Miracles
- **8 verses** (32%) reference virgin birth, miracles, or support by Holy Spirit (Gabriel)
- Miracles affirmed but attributed to God's permission, not Jesus's inherent divinity

### Theme 3: Polemic Against Divinity
- **6 verses** (24%) explicitly reject Trinity, divine sonship, or deification
- Presented as correction of Christian "excess in religion" (*ghuluww*, غلو)

### Theme 4: Crucifixion Denial
- **2 verses** (4:157-158) address crucifixion, both denying Jesus's death on cross
- Most controversial element in Christian-Muslim relations

### Theme 5: Eschatological Role
- **3 verses** (3:55, 4:157-158, 43:61) reference ascension and/or second coming
- Jesus's unique status as only prophet to ascend alive and prophesied to return

## Titles of Jesus in the Quran

Beyond the name "*Isa*" (عيسى), Jesus is given multiple honorific titles:

| **Arabic Title** | **Transliteration** | **English** | **Frequency** | **Significance** |
|-----------------|---------------------|-------------|---------------|------------------|
| **المسيح** | *Al-Masih* | The Messiah | 11 times | Same root as Hebrew *Mashiach* (anointed one) |
| **ابن مريم** | *Ibn Maryam* | Son of Mary | 23 times | Emphasizes human mother, not divine father |
| **كلمة الله** | *Kalimatullah* | Word of God | 1 time (3:45) | Unique to Jesus—Word manifest as person |
| **روح منه** | *Ruhun minhu* | Spirit from Him | 1 time (4:171) | Created spirit from God's command |
| **رسول** | *Rasul* | Messenger | 5 times | Prophet with scripture (Torah-Gospel-Quran) |
| **عبد الله** | *'Abd Allah* | Servant of God | 1 time (19:30) | Jesus's self-description in cradle |
| **آية** | *Ayah* | Sign | 2 times | Jesus as miracle/sign for humanity |
| **وجيه** | *Wajih* | Distinguished/Honored | 1 time (3:45) | Honored in this world and hereafter |
| **من المقربين** | *Min al-muqarrabīn* | Among those near [to Allah] | 1 time (3:45) | High spiritual status |

**Most Significant:** "**Word of God**" (*Kalimatullah*) and "**Spirit from Him**" (*Ruhun minhu*) grant Jesus unique ontological status among prophets, while "**Servant of God**" (*'Abd Allah*) anchors him firmly in created, non-divine status.

## Mary (Maryam) in the Quran

Jesus cannot be understood without his mother, who receives extraordinary honor:

### Mary's Unique Status:
- **Only woman named in the Quran** (mentioned 34 times vs. 19 in NT)
- **Entire surah named after her** (Surah 19: *Maryam*)
- **Model of piety** for all believers (Surah 66:12)
- **Virgin birth affirmed** (Surah 19:20, 21:91)
- **Fed miraculously** while secluded in temple (Surah 3:37)
- **Chosen above all women of the worlds** (Surah 3:42)

### Quranic Nativity Narrative (Surah 19:16-34):
1. Mary withdraws to eastern place (19:16)
2. Angel appears in form of man (19:17)
3. Mary asks, "How can I have a son when no man has touched me?" (19:20)
4. Angel: "Your Lord says, 'It is easy for Me'" (19:21)
5. Mary conceives, withdraws to remote place (19:22)
6. Labor pains drive her to palm tree (19:23)
7. Voice calls: "Shake the trunk, dates will fall" (19:25)
8. Returns to her people carrying infant (19:27)
9. They accuse her of unchastity (19:27-28)
10. **Infant Jesus speaks in her defense** (19:30-33)

**Differences from Luke's Gospel:**
- No census, Bethlehem, manger, shepherds, or Herod
- Birth occurs in isolation under palm tree (not in Bethlehem stable)
- Infant speaks immediately (not in canonical Gospels)
- Dates fall from palm (not in Gospels)

**Similarities:**
- Virgin birth via divine intervention
- Mary's initial fear/confusion
- Angelic announcement
- Jesus's exalted status proclaimed at birth

## Interfaith Significance

### Common Ground Between Christians and Muslims on Jesus:

| **Belief** | **Christianity** | **Islam** | **Shared Foundation** |
|-----------|----------------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Virgin Birth** | Matthew 1:18-25, Luke 1:26-38 | Quran 3:45-47, 19:16-21 | ✅ Both affirm miraculous conception |
| **Miracles** | All four Gospels | Quran 3:49, 5:110 | ✅ Both affirm healing, raising dead, etc. |
| **Messiah** | "Christ" = Messiah | *Al-Masih* = The Messiah | ✅ Both use title, differ on meaning |
| **Ascension** | Acts 1:9-11 | Quran 3:55, 4:158 | ✅ Both teach Jesus ascended |
| **Second Coming** | Matthew 24:30, Acts 1:11 | Hadith tradition | ✅ Both await Jesus's return |
| **Teaching Peace** | Matthew 5:9, John 14:27 | Quran 19:33 (*salaam*) | ✅ Both central to his message |

### Irreconcilable Differences:

| **Doctrine** | **Christianity** | **Islam** | **Why It Matters** |
|-------------|----------------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Divinity** | Jesus = God incarnate | Jesus = created prophet | Core identity of who Jesus is |
| **Trinity** | Father, Son, Holy Spirit | Rejected as *shirk* (polytheism) | Central to Christian worship |
| **Crucifixion** | Atoning sacrifice for sins | Denied; Jesus ascended alive | Foundation of Christian salvation |
| **Resurrection** | Bodily resurrection on third day | Not crucified, so no resurrection | Christian faith depends on it (1 Cor 15:14) |
| **Sonship** | "Son of God" (divine relationship) | Metaphorical only; God has no offspring | Trinitarian theology vs. absolute monotheism |

## Bridge-Building for Interfaith Dialogue

Despite irreconcilable doctrinal differences, Christians and Muslims can:

### 1. Acknowledge Shared Reverence
- Both traditions hold Jesus in highest esteem (Christians: as divine Savior; Muslims: as one of five greatest prophets)
- Both affirm his moral teachings as authoritative
- Both await his eschatological role

### 2. Focus on Shared Ethical Teachings
- Sermon on the Mount (Matthew 5-7) resonates with Quranic ethics
- "Love your neighbor" (Matthew 22:39) parallels "None of you believes until he loves for his brother what he loves for himself" (Hadith)
- Peace (*shalom* / *salaam*) as central value

### 3. Study Historical Context of Quranic Polemic
- Quran's anti-Trinitarian passages addressed specific 7th-century Arabian Christian sects, not Nicene orthodoxy
- Understanding historical context reduces perception of direct attack on modern Christianity

### 4. Explore the "Third Way" - Prophetic Tradition
- Judaism, Christianity, and Islam share prophetic tradition: God speaks through chosen messengers
- Jesus fits this paradigm in all three traditions (Christianity adds divine nature)

### 5. Collaborate on Shared Values
- Work together on peace-building, poverty alleviation, social justice
- Jesus's teaching "by their fruits you will know them" (Matthew 7:16) and Quran's "vie with one another in good works" (5:48) support cooperative action

## Conclusion: "The Word of Truth" (Surah 19:34)

The Quran calls Jesus **"the word of truth about which they are in dispute"** (19:34)—a profound acknowledgment that Jesus's identity has been contested for centuries. This article has presented all 25 Quranic mentions of Jesus with scholarly rigor, allowing readers to understand the Islamic perspective from primary sources.

### Key Takeaways:

1. **Jesus is profoundly honored in Islam** as virgin-born Messiah, miracle-worker, and one of the five greatest prophets (*Ulul-'Azm*)

2. **The Quran affirms Jesus's virgin birth, miracles, ascension, and second coming**—doctrines rejected by secular scholarship but embraced by both Muslims and Christians (though with different theological interpretations)

3. **The crucifixion denial (Surah 4:157) remains the deepest divide**, rooted in fundamentally different understandings of divine justice, prophetic protection, and redemptive suffering

4. **Quranic Christology is coherent within Islamic monotheistic framework**—every verse emphasizes Jesus's prophethood, humanity, and complete submission (*islam*) to God

5. **Interfaith dialogue requires understanding, not agreement**—Muslims and Christians can respect each other's deepest convictions while maintaining theological differences
