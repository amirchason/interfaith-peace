## Introduction

**Surprising fact:** Jesus (*Isa* in Arabic, عيسى) is mentioned by name in the Quran **25 times**—more frequently than the Prophet Muhammad himself (mentioned by name only 4 times). Mary (*Maryam*, مريم), Jesus's mother, appears more often in the Quran than in the entire New Testament, and an entire chapter (Surah 19) bears her name.

For many Christians, discovering that Muslims revere Jesus comes as a shock. For many Muslims, Christians are surprised to learn the depth of Islamic honor for Jesus. And for both communities, these shared reverence offers profound common ground for dialogue, mutual understanding, and collaborative peacemaking.

This article explores the Islamic understanding of Jesus through:
- **Quranic verses** about Jesus with Arabic text and translations
- **Jesus's titles** in Islam: Prophet, Messiah, Word of God, Sign to humanity
- **Miracles and teachings** attributed to Jesus in the Quran
- **Similarities** between Christian and Islamic views of Jesus
- **Differences** explained with theological sensitivity
- **Jesus as peacemaker** in both traditions
- **Implications** for Christian-Muslim relations and interfaith peace

Whether you're Christian seeking to understand Muslim neighbors, Muslim wanting deeper knowledge of your own tradition's Jesus, or simply interested in interfaith dialogue, this guide provides scholarly, balanced insight into one of history's most significant figures as understood in Islam.

## Jesus (Isa) in the Quran: Quick Overview

### Essential Facts About Jesus in Islam

**What do Muslims believe about Jesus?**

Islam teaches that Jesus (Arabic: *Isa ibn Maryam*, عيسى ابن مريم, "Jesus son of Mary") was:

1. **A prophet and messenger of God** (*nabi* and *rasul*)—one of the greatest prophets, in the lineage of Abraham, Moses, and Muhammad
2. **Born of the Virgin Mary** through miraculous conception without a father
3. **The Messiah** (*Al-Masih*, المسيح)—anointed by God for special mission
4. **A miracle-worker** who healed the sick, raised the dead, and spoke from the cradle
5. **A teacher of righteousness** who called people to worship One God
6. **Ascended to heaven** alive (not crucified according to Quran's interpretation)
7. **Expected to return** before the Day of Judgment

**What Muslims do NOT believe:**
- Jesus is NOT divine (not God or "Son of God" in literal sense)
- Jesus was NOT crucified (Quranic interpretation differs from Christian understanding)
- Jesus did NOT atone for sin through death (Islam emphasizes personal accountability)
- Trinity is NOT accepted (strict monotheism, *tawhid*)

These differences are theologically significant, but the shared reverence for Jesus as righteous prophet and his mother Mary creates remarkable common ground.

### Titles and Honors of Jesus in the Quran

The Quran bestows extraordinary titles upon Jesus:

| **Arabic Title** | **Translation** | **Quranic Reference** | **Significance** |
|------------------|-----------------|----------------------|------------------|
| **'Isa** (عيسى) | Jesus | Throughout | His personal name |
| **Al-Masih** (المسيح) | The Messiah/Christ | Surah 3:45, 4:171-172 | Anointed one (same as Greek *Christos*) |
| **Ruh-Allah** (روح الله) | Spirit from God | Surah 4:171 | Unique spiritual status |
| **Kalimatullah** (كلمة الله) | Word of God | Surah 3:45, 4:171 | Divine Word made manifest |
| **'Abd-Allah** (عبد الله) | Servant of God | Surah 19:30 | Jesus's self-identification |
| **Rasul** (رسول) | Messenger | Surah 5:75 | Sent with divine message |
| **Nabi** (نبي) | Prophet | Throughout | Inspired by God |
| **Ayah** (آية) | Sign | Surah 19:21, 23:50 | Miraculous proof of God's power |

The titles *Messiah* and *Word of God* are particularly striking—Muslims affirm Jesus as the Messiah prophesied in Hebrew scripture, and as God's Word (though interpreting this differently than Christians).

## Quranic Verses About Jesus: Comprehensive Collection

### The Annunciation: Angels Announce Jesus to Mary

**Surah 3:45-47 - Jesus Called Messiah, Word from God**

> **Arabic:** إِذْ قَالَتِ ٱلْمَلَـٰٓئِكَةُ يَـٰمَرْيَمُ إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ يُبَشِّرُكِ بِكَلِمَةٍۢ مِّنْهُ ٱسْمُهُ ٱلْمَسِيحُ عِيسَى ٱبْنُ مَرْيَمَ وَجِيهًۭا فِى ٱلدُّنْيَا وَٱلْـَٔاخِرَةِ وَمِنَ ٱلْمُقَرَّبِينَ
>
> **Transliteration:** *Idh qālat al-malā'ikatu yā Maryamu inna Allāha yubashshiruki bi-kalimatin minhu ismuhu al-Masīḥu 'Īsā ibnu Maryam wajīhan fī ad-dunyā wa-al-ākhirati wa-mina al-muqarrabīn*
>
> **Translation:** "When the angels said, 'O Mary, indeed Allah gives you good news of **a Word from Him**, whose name will be **the Messiah, Jesus, son of Mary**—honored in this world and the Hereafter and among those brought near [to Allah]. He will speak to people in the cradle and in maturity and will be of the righteous.' She said, 'My Lord, how can I have a child when no man has touched me?' He said, 'Such is Allah; He creates what He wills. When He decrees a matter, He only says to it, "Be," and it is.'" (Surah 3:45-47, Sahih International)

**Key elements:**
- **Angels announce:** Similar to Luke 1:26-38 (Gabriel's announcement to Mary)
- **"Word from Him" (Kalimatullah):** Jesus is God's creative word—"Be, and it is"
- **Messiah:** Explicit affirmation that Jesus is the Messiah
- **Virgin conception:** Mary asks, "How can I have a child...?" Divine response: miraculous creation
- **Speak in cradle:** Prophecy of infant Jesus defending Mary (elaborated in Surah 19)

---

**Surah 19:16-21 - Virgin Birth Account**

> **Arabic:** وَٱذْكُرْ فِى ٱلْكِتَـٰبِ مَرْيَمَ إِذِ ٱنتَبَذَتْ مِنْ أَهْلِهَا مَكَانًۭا شَرْقِيًّۭا فَٱتَّخَذَتْ مِن دُونِهِمْ حِجَابًۭا فَأَرْسَلْنَآ إِلَيْهَا رُوحَنَا فَتَمَثَّلَ لَهَا بَشَرًۭا سَوِيًّۭا
>
> **Transliteration:** *Wadhkur fī al-kitābi Maryam idh intabadhat min ahlihā makānan sharqiyyan fa-ittakhadhat min dūnihim ḥijāban fa-arsalnā ilayhā rūḥanā fa-tamathala lahā basharan sawiyyā*
>
> **Translation:** "And mention, [O Muhammad], in the Book [the story of] Mary, when she withdrew from her family to a place toward the east. And she took, in seclusion from them, a screen. Then We sent to her Our Spirit [Angel Gabriel], and he represented himself to her as a well-proportioned man. She said, 'Indeed, I seek refuge in the Most Merciful from you, so leave me be if you fear Allah.' He said, 'I am only the messenger of your Lord to give you [news of] a pure boy.' She said, 'How can I have a boy while no man has touched me and I have not been unchaste?' He said, 'Thus [it will be]; your Lord says, It is easy for Me, and We will make him **a sign to the people** and a mercy from Us. And it is a matter [already] decreed.'" (Surah 19:16-21)

**Islamic understanding of virgin birth:**
- **No biological father:** Jesus's conception was purely miraculous
- **"Be, and it is":** Allah's creative power needs no human agency
- **Sign to humanity:** The virgin birth demonstrates God's sovereignty over natural law
- **Parallel to Adam:** Surah 3:59 compares Jesus to Adam—both created without normal process (Adam without parents, Jesus without father)

**Similarity to Christian belief:** Both Islam and Christianity affirm the virgin birth as historical fact and divine miracle. This is one of the strongest areas of agreement.

### Jesus's Miracles in the Quran

**Surah 3:49 - Jesus Performs Miracles**

> **Arabic:** وَرَسُولًا إِلَىٰ بَنِىٓ إِسْرَٰٓءِيلَ أَنِّى قَدْ جِئْتُكُم بِـَٔايَةٍۢ مِّن رَّبِّكُمْ ۖ أَنِّىٓ أَخْلُقُ لَكُم مِّنَ ٱلطِّينِ كَهَيْـَٔةِ ٱلطَّيْرِ فَأَنفُخُ فِيهِ فَيَكُونُ طَيْرًۢا بِإِذْنِ ٱللَّهِ ۖ وَأُبْرِئُ ٱلْأَكْمَهَ وَٱلْأَبْرَصَ وَأُحْىِ ٱلْمَوْتَىٰ بِإِذْنِ ٱللَّهِ
>
> **Transliteration:** *Wa-rasūlan ilā banī Isrā'īl annī qad ji'tukum bi-āyatin min rabbikum annī akhluqu lakum mina aṭ-ṭīni ka-hay'ati aṭ-ṭayri fa-anfukhu fīhi fa-yakūnu ṭayran bi-idhni Allāh, wa-ubri'u al-akmaha wa-al-abraṣa wa-uḥyī al-mawtā bi-idhni Allāh*
>
> **Translation:** "[Jesus said,] 'And [I am] a messenger to the Children of Israel [announcing], Indeed I have come to you with a sign from your Lord: I **design for you from clay the likeness of a bird, then I breathe into it and it becomes a bird by permission of Allah. And I cure the blind and the leper, and I give life to the dead, by permission of Allah**. And I inform you of what you eat and what you store in your houses. Indeed in that is a sign for you, if you are believers.'" (Surah 3:49)

**Miracles affirmed:**
1. **Creating bird from clay:** Unique to Quran, appears in apocryphal Infancy Gospel of Thomas
2. **Healing blind:** Parallels John 9
3. **Healing leprosy:** Parallels Matthew 8:1-4
4. **Raising dead:** Parallels John 11 (Lazarus), Luke 7:11-17 (widow's son)
5. **Knowledge of hidden things:** Knowing what people eat/store (parallel to John 4:16-18, Jesus knowing Samaritan woman's history)

**Critical phrase:** "By permission of Allah" (*bi-idhni Allāh*) appears repeatedly. Islam emphasizes Jesus performed miracles through divine empowerment, not his own inherent divinity. Prophets are channels of God's power, not gods themselves.

**Surah 5:110 - God Reminds Jesus of His Miracles**

> "When Allah will say, 'O Jesus, Son of Mary, remember My favor upon you and upon your mother when I supported you with the Holy Spirit and you spoke to the people in the cradle and in maturity; and when I taught you writing and wisdom and the Torah and the Gospel; and when you designed from clay the likeness of a bird with My permission, then you breathed into it, and it became a bird with My permission; and you healed the blind and the leper with My permission; and when you brought forth the dead with My permission...'"

This verse (spoken by God on Day of Judgment) catalogs Jesus's extraordinary gifts—affirming their historical reality while attributing them to divine enabling.

### Jesus Speaks from the Cradle

**Surah 19:27-33 - Infant Jesus Defends Mary**

> **Arabic:** فَأَشَارَتْ إِلَيْهِ ۖ قَالُوا۟ كَيْفَ نُكَلِّمُ مَن كَانَ فِى ٱلْمَهْدِ صَبِيًّۭا قَالَ إِنِّى عَبْدُ ٱللَّهِ ءَاتَىٰنِىَ ٱلْكِتَـٰبَ وَجَعَلَنِى نَبِيًّۭا وَجَعَلَنِى مُبَارَكًا أَيْنَ مَا كُنتُ وَأَوْصَـٰنِى بِٱلصَّلَوٰةِ وَٱلزَّكَوٰةِ مَا دُمْتُ حَيًّۭا وَبَرًّۢا بِوَٰلِدَتِى وَلَمْ يَجْعَلْنِى جَبَّارًۭا شَقِيًّۭا وَٱلسَّلَـٰمُ عَلَىَّ يَوْمَ وُلِدتُّ وَيَوْمَ أَمُوتُ وَيَوْمَ أُبْعَثُ حَيًّۭا
>
> **Transliteration:** *Qāla innī 'abdu Allāh ātāniya al-kitāba wa-ja'alanī nabiyyā, wa-ja'alanī mubārakan ayna mā kuntu wa-awṣānī bi-aṣ-ṣalāti wa-az-zakāti mā dumtu ḥayyā, wa-barran bi-wālidatī wa-lam yaj'alnī jabbāran shaqiyyā, wa-as-salāmu 'alayya yawma wulidtu wa-yawma amūtu wa-yawma ub'athu ḥayyā*
>
> **Translation:** "So she pointed to him. They said, 'How can we speak to one who is in the cradle, a child?' [Jesus] said, '**Indeed, I am the servant of Allah. He has given me the Scripture and made me a prophet. And He has made me blessed wherever I am and has enjoined upon me prayer and zakah [charity] as long as I remain alive, and [made me] dutiful to my mother, and He has not made me a wretched tyrant. And peace (salaam) be upon me the day I was born and the day I die and the day I am raised alive.**'" (Surah 19:30-33)

**Narrative context:**

Mary returns to her people carrying infant Jesus. They accuse her of unchastity. Unable to defend herself verbally (she had been commanded to fast from speech), Mary points to the baby. Miraculously, Jesus speaks:

**Jesus's declaration:**
1. **"I am the servant of Allah"** (*'abd-Allah*)—first words establish his prophethood, not divinity
2. **Given Scripture and prophethood**—even as infant, divinely commissioned
3. **Commanded prayer and charity**—core Islamic practices
4. **Dutiful to mother**—emphasizes Mary's honor
5. **"Peace upon me"** (*salaam*)—Jesus's very identity associated with peace

This miracle (infant speech) vindicates Mary's virtue and establishes Jesus's prophetic authority from birth. No biblical parallel exists, though apocryphal Christian texts mention similar stories.

### Jesus's Message and Ministry

**Surah 5:46 - Jesus Given the Gospel**

> "And We sent, following in their footsteps, Jesus, son of Mary, confirming that which came before him in the Torah; and We gave him **the Gospel (Injeel)**, in which was guidance and light and confirming that which preceded it of the Torah as guidance and instruction for the righteous."

**Islamic understanding of Injeel:**
- *Injeel* (الإنجيل) = Gospel, derived from Greek *euangelion* (good news)
- In Islam, the *Injeel* is a revelation given directly to Jesus—not the four canonical Gospels (Matthew, Mark, Luke, John), which Muslims view as human accounts, some accurate and some altered
- Muslims believe the original *Injeel* confirmed Torah's message of strict monotheism and predicted Muhammad

This creates tension in interfaith dialogue—Muslims honor "Gospel" but understand it differently than Christians.

---

**Surah 61:6 - Jesus Prophesies Muhammad**

> "And [mention] when Jesus, son of Mary, said, 'O Children of Israel, indeed I am the messenger of Allah to you confirming what came before me of the Torah and bringing good news of a messenger to come after me, whose name is Ahmad.'"

**Context:**
- *Ahmad* (أحمد) = "the praised one," related etymologically to *Muhammad* (محمد)
- Muslims interpret this as Jesus prophesying Muhammad's coming
- Christians do not find this in canonical Gospels; some Muslims point to John 14-16's *Paraclete* (Comforter/Advocate) as possible reference

**Interfaith sensitivity:** This verse is theologically fraught. Christians see the Paraclete as Holy Spirit; Muslims see it as Muhammad. Respectful dialogue acknowledges different interpretations without denigrating either tradition.

### Jesus's Ascension (Not Crucifixion)

**Surah 4:157-158 - Islamic Understanding of Jesus's End**

> **Arabic:** وَقَوْلِهِمْ إِنَّا قَتَلْنَا ٱلْمَسِيحَ عِيسَى ٱبْنَ مَرْيَمَ رَسُولَ ٱللَّهِ وَمَا قَتَلُوهُ وَمَا صَلَبُوهُ وَلَـٰكِن شُبِّهَ لَهُمْ ۚ وَإِنَّ ٱلَّذِينَ ٱخْتَلَفُوا۟ فِيهِ لَفِى شَكٍّۢ مِّنْهُ ۚ مَا لَهُم بِهِۦ مِنْ عِلْمٍ إِلَّا ٱتِّبَاعَ ٱلظَّنِّ ۚ وَمَا قَتَلُوهُ يَقِينًۢا بَل رَّفَعَهُ ٱللَّهُ إِلَيْهِ
>
> **Transliteration:** *Wa-qawlihim innā qatalnā al-Masīḥa 'Īsā ibna Maryam rasūla Allāh, wa-mā qatalūhu wa-mā ṣalabūhu wa-lākin shubbiha lahum... bal rafa'ahu Allāhu ilayh*
>
> **Translation:** "And [for] their saying, 'Indeed, we have killed the Messiah, Jesus son of Mary, the messenger of Allah.' **And they did not kill him, nor did they crucify him; but it was made to appear so to them**... Rather, **Allah raised him to Himself**. And ever is Allah Exalted in Might and Wise." (Surah 4:157-158)

**Islamic interpretation:**
- **Jesus was not crucified:** Someone else (Judas, a volunteer, or a likeness) was crucified in Jesus's place
- **Allah raised Jesus alive to heaven:** Jesus did not die but was taken up bodily
- **Jesus will return:** Islamic eschatology teaches Jesus will descend before Day of Judgment, defeat the Antichrist (*Dajjal*), establish justice, then die a natural death

**Theological implications:**
- **No atonement:** Since Jesus didn't die on cross, there's no substitutionary atonement for sin (Islam emphasizes personal accountability)
- **No resurrection:** Since Jesus didn't die, no need for resurrection (though he will eventually die and be resurrected with all humanity)

**Scholarly theories on Quranic account:**
- Some suggest Quran corrects Jewish polemic that they killed Jesus (response: "No, Allah exalted him")
- Others propose Docetism influence (early Christian heresy teaching Jesus only *appeared* to suffer)
- Still others see it as Quranic theological correction emphasizing God wouldn't allow His prophet to be humiliated

**Most significant Christian-Muslim difference:** The crucifixion and its meaning is the single largest theological divergence. Christians see the cross as central; Muslims see it as misunderstanding of what actually happened.

## Similarities Between Christian and Islamic Views of Jesus

Despite crucial differences, remarkable commonalities exist. This table maps shared beliefs:

| **Belief** | **Christianity** | **Islam** | **Common Ground** |
|-----------|------------------|----------|-------------------|
| **Virgin Birth** | ✓ Affirmed (Matthew 1, Luke 1-2) | ✓ Affirmed (Surah 3:45-47, 19:16-21) | Both traditions teach miraculous conception without human father |
| **Miracles** | ✓ Healing, exorcisms, raising dead (all 4 Gospels) | ✓ Healing blind/leper, raising dead, bird from clay (Surah 3:49, 5:110) | Both affirm Jesus performed supernatural acts through divine power |
| **Messiah** | ✓ Jesus is *Christos* (Anointed One) | ✓ Jesus is *Al-Masih* (The Messiah/Anointed) | Both traditions use same title (Greek vs. Arabic equivalent) |
| **Word of God** | ✓ Jesus is *Logos* (John 1:1) | ✓ Jesus is *Kalimatullah* (Word from Allah, Surah 3:45) | Both call Jesus "Word," though understanding differs |
| **Sinlessness** | ✓ Jesus without sin (2 Cor 5:21, Heb 4:15) | ✓ Jesus protected from Satan (Hadith: only Jesus and Mary untouched by Satan at birth) | Both traditions emphasize Jesus's moral purity |
| **Teaching of Love** | ✓ Love God, love neighbor (Matt 22:37-40) | ✓ Quran describes Jesus teaching *birr* (righteousness/goodness) | Both portray Jesus teaching ethical conduct and devotion |
| **Ascension** | ✓ Ascended to heaven (Acts 1:9-11) | ✓ Raised alive to Allah (Surah 4:158) | Both teach Jesus currently in heaven (though mechanism differs) |
| **Second Coming** | ✓ Jesus will return (Acts 1:11, Rev 19) | ✓ Jesus will return before Day of Judgment (Hadith literature) | Both anticipate Jesus's future role in end times |
| **Mother Mary** | ✓ Perpetual virgin (Catholic/Orthodox), honored (all traditions) | ✓ Greatest woman ever, virgin, honored (Surah 3:42-43) | Both highly revere Mary; Quran has more Marian content than NT |
| **Born in Bethlehem** | ✓ (Matthew 2, Luke 2) | Implied but not explicit in Quran | General agreement on birthplace |
| **Followers (Disciples)** | ✓ Twelve disciples | ✓ *Hawariyyun* (disciples/helpers) mentioned (Surah 3:52, 61:14) | Both affirm Jesus had devoted followers |

### Why These Similarities Matter

**For Christians:** Understanding Muslims honor Jesus deeply can open doors to respectful conversation. Muslims aren't "rejecting" Jesus—they're honoring him differently than Christians do, but still profoundly.

**For Muslims:** Recognizing Christians' devotion to Jesus (even if expressed through doctrines Islam doesn't accept) can build empathy. Christians aren't "polytheists"—they're trying to honor Jesus as Muslims do, through their theological lens.

**For interfaith work:** These commonalities provide starting points for dialogue—"We both love Jesus; let's explore our different understandings with curiosity rather than hostility."

## Differences Between Christian and Islamic Views of Jesus

Honest interfaith dialogue requires acknowledging differences alongside similarities. These are respectfully presented:

| **Doctrine** | **Christianity** | **Islam** | **Nature of Difference** |
|-------------|------------------|-----------|-------------------------|
| **Divinity** | Jesus is God incarnate (John 1:1, 14; John 10:30) | Jesus is human prophet, NOT divine (Surah 5:72, 19:30) | **Fundamental:** Christianity's central claim; Islam's key rejection |
| **Son of God** | Jesus is God's Son (literal or metaphorical depending on tradition) | Jesus is God's servant/*'abd*; "son" language rejected as polytheistic (Surah 19:35) | **Core theological:** Christian identity; Islamic objection to anthropomorphism |
| **Trinity** | One God in three persons: Father, Son, Holy Spirit | Strict monotheism (*tawhid*); Trinity seen as polytheism (Surah 4:171, 5:73) | **Categorical:** Christianity monotheism-in-trinity; Islam absolute oneness |
| **Crucifixion** | Jesus died on cross (all 4 Gospels, Paul) | Jesus not crucified; substitute died or made to appear so (Surah 4:157) | **Historical/theological:** Different accounts of Jesus's fate |
| **Atonement** | Jesus's death atones for sin (Rom 3:25, 1 John 2:2) | No atonement; each person accountable for own sin (Surah 6:164) | **Salvific:** Christianity's gospel core; Islam's personal responsibility |
| **Resurrection** | Jesus rose bodily from dead third day (1 Cor 15) | Jesus didn't die, so no resurrection (raised alive to heaven) | **Easter event:** Central Christian claim; not part of Islamic narrative |
| **Nature** | Fully God and fully human (Council of Chalcedon) | Fully human, prophet chosen by God | **Christological:** Christian two-natures doctrine; Islamic prophetology |
| **Worship** | Worship Jesus as Lord and God (Phil 2:9-11) | Worship only Allah; Jesus worshipped Allah (Surah 5:116-117) | **Practical:** Christians pray to Jesus; Muslims forbid praying to anyone but Allah |
| **Titles** | Lord, Savior, Redeemer | Prophet, Messenger, Servant of Allah | **Vocabulary:** Reflect different understandings of Jesus's role |
| **Scripture** | Jesus fulfills Hebrew Bible; NT reveals him | Jesus confirms Torah, given *Injeel* (original Gospel, now altered per Islam) | **Canon:** Different understandings of which books are authoritative |

### Understanding the Differences Theologically

**Christian perspective:**

For Christians, Jesus's divinity, crucifixion, and resurrection are non-negotiable—without these, Christianity collapses (1 Corinthians 15:14: "If Christ has not been raised, our preaching is useless"). Christians see Islamic view of Jesus as honoring him but missing his true identity and salvific work.

**Islamic perspective:**

For Muslims, *tawhid* (absolute oneness of God) is non-negotiable. Any suggestion that Jesus is divine contradicts the *Shahada* (Islamic creed): "There is no god but Allah." Muslims see Christian Trinitarian doctrine as inadvertently compromising monotheism, even if Christians intend otherwise. Islam views itself as restoring Jesus's true, monotheistic message.

**Can these be reconciled?**

Theologically, no—these are genuinely incompatible claims about ultimate reality. **But** Christians and Muslims can:
- **Respect each tradition's internal integrity:** Each view is coherent within its own theological framework
- **Acknowledge good faith:** Neither tradition is intentionally "distorting" Jesus; both sincerely honor him
- **Focus on shared ethics:** Both follow Jesus's (and Muhammad's) calls to love, justice, mercy, peace
- **Collaborate on shared values:** Work together for peace, justice, care for poor—embodying what both traditions teach

## Jesus as Peacemaker: Quran and Bible Comparison

One of the most beautiful areas of convergence is Jesus's association with peace in both traditions.

### Jesus's Peace Teachings: Side by Side

| **Theme** | **Quran** | **Bible** | **Shared Message** |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Peace as Identity** | Surah 19:33: Jesus says, "*Peace (salaam) on me* the day I was born" | Luke 2:14: Angels announce Jesus's birth: "*Peace on earth*" | Jesus's very existence brings peace |
| **Call to Repentance** | Surah 3:50-51: Jesus calls Israel to worship God and obey | Matthew 4:17: "Repent, for kingdom of heaven is near" | Both emphasize turning toward God |
| **Compassion for Weak** | Surah 19:32: Jesus made dutiful to his mother, not "tyrant" | Matthew 9:36: Jesus has compassion on crowds, "harassed and helpless" | Jesus champions vulnerable |
| **Righteousness** | Surah 3:46: Jesus speaks "in maturity" teaching right conduct | Matthew 5-7: Sermon on the Mount—Jesus's ethical teachings | Both portray Jesus as moral teacher |
| **Love Over Law** | Islam emphasizes Jesus restored mercy to overly legalistic Judaism | Mark 2:27: "Sabbath made for man, not man for Sabbath" | Jesus prioritizes human welfare |
| **Miracles of Mercy** | Surah 3:49: Jesus heals blind, leper, raises dead | Gospels throughout: Jesus's healing ministry | Divine compassion shown through Jesus |

### Jesus and Peacemaking in Christian Tradition

**Jesus explicitly teaches peace:**

- **Matthew 5:9:** "Blessed are the peacemakers, for they will be called children of God"
- **Matthew 5:38-42:** Turn the other cheek (active nonviolent resistance)
- **Matthew 5:43-48:** Love your enemies
- **John 14:27:** "Peace I leave with you; my peace I give you"
- **Matthew 26:52:** "All who draw the sword will die by the sword" (rejecting violence even in self-defense)

Christians see Jesus as the "Prince of Peace" (Isaiah 9:6) who broke cycles of violence through suffering love. His crucifixion (in Christian understanding) is the ultimate peacemaking act—reconciling humanity to God and each other.

See our article: [Jesus's Teachings on Peace](/articles/jesus-teachings-peace) for comprehensive exploration.

### Jesus and Peacemaking in Islamic Tradition

**Quran emphasizes Jesus's gentleness:**

- **Surah 19:32:** Jesus says God "has not made me a *jabbar* (tyrant/oppressor)"
- **Surah 57:27:** "We placed in hearts of Jesus's followers compassion and mercy"
- **Hadith:** Muhammad said, "Do you know who are the first to enter Paradise? The poor and humble"—embodied by Jesus

Muslims see Jesus (*Isa*) as exemplar of humility, devotion, and gentleness. Though not central to Islamic peace ethics (as Muhammad's example is primary), Jesus's character reinforces Islamic values of mercy (*rahma*) and kindness.

**Jesus bridges Christian-Muslim peacemaking:**

When Christians and Muslims work together for peace—opposing war, serving refugees, building justice—they're honoring both Jesus and Muhammad. Both prophets taught peace; both communities claim this heritage. Collaborative peacemaking becomes interfaith witness.

## Why Jesus in Islam Matters for Christian-Muslim Relations

### Historical Context: Centuries of Misunderstanding

For much of history, Christians and Muslims knew little about each other's actual beliefs:

- **Medieval Christians** often viewed Muslims as pagans or heretics, unaware Muslims honored Jesus
- **Medieval Muslims** sometimes viewed Christians as polytheists, misunderstanding Trinity
- **Crusades, colonialism, and ongoing conflicts** deepened mutual suspicion and hostility

**Modern reality:** Most Christians still don't know Muslims revere Jesus. Most Muslims don't understand why Christians call Jesus "Son of God" without meaning biological son.

### Discovering Common Ground: Transformative Potential

**When Christians learn Muslims honor Jesus:**

"I was shocked to discover Muslims believe in the virgin birth! I thought they rejected Jesus entirely. Now I see we have more in common than I realized." —Christian seminary student after interfaith dialogue

**When Muslims learn Christians' Trinitarian monotheism:**

"I grew up thinking Christians worship three gods. Learning they insist on one God, expressed in three persons, helped me see their sincerity, even if I still disagree theologically." —Muslim interfaith activist

**When both recognize shared reverence despite different interpretations:**

Dialogue doesn't require agreement. It requires:
- **Accurate understanding** of what the other actually believes
- **Respect** for the other's sincerity and internal logic
- **Humility** to admit limits of one's own perspective
- **Focus on shared values** (love, justice, mercy, peace) that both can practice together

### Practical Applications for Interfaith Peace

**1. Joint service projects:**

Christians and Muslims can collaborate feeding hungry, housing homeless, caring for refugees—honoring both Jesus and Muhammad's commands to serve the vulnerable.

**2. Interfaith dialogue events:**

"Jesus in Christianity and Islam" is an excellent dialogue topic—shared reverence provides common ground, differences spark rich conversation.

**3. Peacebuilding in conflict zones:**

In places with Christian-Muslim tension, reminding both communities of their shared love for Jesus can reduce hostility. Example: Nigerian Imam Ashafa and Pastor James Wuye, former enemies, now partners in peace, cite both Jesus and Muhammad as their inspiration.

**4. Educational initiatives:**

Churches teaching about Islam and mosques teaching about Christianity reduce fear born of ignorance. Simply learning "Muslims love Jesus too" can transform attitudes.

**5. Shared prayer for peace:**

While Christians and Muslims can't pray *together* in worship (different understandings of God), they can pray *alongside* each other for peace, each in their own tradition. Shared silence or alternating prayers honors both traditions.

## Conclusion: Jesus the Bridge

Jesus stands at the crossroads of Christianity and Islam. For Christians, he is Lord and Savior. For Muslims, he is beloved prophet. These are not the same—but neither are they utterly incompatible. Both communities revere him, both seek to follow his teachings (as each understands them), and both can honor his call to peace.

### What We've Learned

**Islam deeply honors Jesus:**
- Virgin-born Messiah
- Worker of miracles
- Word of God
- Pure and sinless
- Ascended to heaven
- Returning before end times

**Christians and Muslims agree on much:**
- Virgin birth, miracles, messiahship, ascension, second coming
- Both call Jesus "Word of God" and "Messiah"
- Both revere Mary as greatest of women
- Both see Jesus teaching love, mercy, justice

**Christians and Muslims differ significantly:**
- Divinity (yes/no)
- Crucifixion (yes/no)
- Atonement (yes/no)
- Trinity (yes/no)

**These differences need not prevent collaboration:**
- Shared ethics enable shared action
- Mutual respect doesn't require theological agreement
- Jesus calls both communities to peacemaking
