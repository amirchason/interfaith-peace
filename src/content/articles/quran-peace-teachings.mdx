## The Meaning of "Peace" in Islam

### Quick Answer: What Does Peace Mean in Islam?

**Peace** (*Salaam*, سلام) in Islam derives from the trilateral Semitic root **S-L-M** (س-ل-م), which conveys submission, safety, wholeness, and peace. This root generates multiple interconnected meanings:

- **Salaam:** Peace, safety, security, well-being
- **Islam:** Submission to God, surrender to divine will (which produces peace)
- **Muslim:** One who submits to God, one who enters into peace
- **Aslama:** To surrender, to make peace, to enter into safety

Peace in Islam is thus intrinsically connected to *tawhid* (God's oneness) and *taqwa* (God-consciousness). True peace arises from aligning human will with divine purpose, resulting in inner tranquility, just social relations, and ultimately the peace of paradise.

### As-Salaam: God as the Source of Peace

The Quran identifies Allah with peace itself:

**Surah 59:23**

> **Arabic:** هُوَ ٱللَّهُ ٱلَّذِى لَآ إِلَـٰهَ إِلَّا هُوَ ٱلْمَلِكُ ٱلْقُدُّوسُ ٱلسَّلَـٰمُ
>
> **Transliteration:** *Huwa Allāhu alladhī lā ilāha illā huwa, al-Maliku al-Quddūsu as-Salām*
>
> **Translation:** "He is Allah—there is no god except Him: the King, the Most Holy, **the Peace** (*As-Salaam*), the Giver of Security, the Guardian of Faith, the Almighty, the Supreme in Might, the Majestic. Glorified is Allah far above what they associate with Him." (Surah 59:23, Sahih International)

*As-Salaam* is one of the 99 Beautiful Names of Allah (*Asma ul-Husna*). Classical Islamic scholars interpreted this name in multiple ways:

- **Peace itself:** Allah is the essence and source of all peace
- **Giver of peace:** Allah bestows peace upon creation
- **Free from imperfection:** Allah is perfect, complete, whole (parallel to Hebrew *shalom*)
- **Safe from injustice:** Allah never wrongs anyone; all His actions are just

When Muslims invoke *As-Salaam* in prayer or remembrance (*dhikr*), they're acknowledging that authentic peace flows only from divine presence. Human efforts at peacemaking, therefore, align with God's own nature.

### Salaam and Shalom: Linguistic Cousins

Arabic *Salaam* (سلام) and Hebrew *Shalom* (שָׁלוֹם) are not merely similar—they're linguistic siblings from the same Semitic root. This profound connection reflects the shared heritage of Abrahamic faiths.

**Comparison:**

| **Arabic** | **Hebrew** | **Shared Meaning** |
|------------|------------|-------------------|
| Salaam (سلام) | Shalom (שָׁלוֹם) | Peace, wholeness, safety |
| As-salamu alaykum | Shalom aleichem | Peace be upon you |
| Dar al-Salaam | Ir Shalom | City/House of Peace |
| Muslimūn (peaceful ones) | Shlemim (complete/whole ones) | Those in peace |

When Jews and Muslims greet each other with *"Shalom"* or *"Salaam,"* they're using nearly identical words invoking the same reality: comprehensive well-being rooted in divine blessing.

### Paradise as Dar al-Salaam

The Quran repeatedly describes paradise as *Dar al-Salaam*, the "Abode of Peace":

**Surah 10:25**

> **Arabic:** وَٱللَّهُ يَدْعُوٓا۟ إِلَىٰ دَارِ ٱلسَّلَـٰمِ
>
> **Transliteration:** *Wallāhu yad'ū ilā dāri as-salām*
>
> **Translation:** "And Allah invites to the Home of Peace and guides whom He wills to a straight path." (Surah 10:25)

**Surah 6:127**

> **Arabic:** لَهُمْ دَارُ ٱلسَّلَـٰمِ عِندَ رَبِّهِمْ
>
> **Transliteration:** *Lahum dāru as-salāmi 'inda rabbihim*
>
> **Translation:** "For them will be the Home of Peace with their Lord." (Surah 6:127)

In paradise, peace is absolute and eternal:
- No conflict, no sorrow, no fear
- Complete safety and security
- Perfect relationships
- Divine presence (greatest blessing)

The Quran even describes the very greeting of paradise as peace:

**Surah 56:25-26**

> "They will not hear therein ill speech or commission of sin—only a saying: *'Peace, peace (Salāmun, Salām).'*"

This vision of ultimate peace gives Muslims a eschatological hope that shapes present ethics—if paradise is characterized by peace, believers should embody peace now as foretaste of future glory.

## Key Quranic Verses on Peace

### Peace as Divine Command

**Surah 8:61 - If They Incline to Peace, You Also Incline to It**

> **Arabic:** وَإِن جَنَحُوا۟ لِلسَّلْمِ فَٱجْنَحْ لَهَا وَتَوَكَّلْ عَلَى ٱللَّهِ ۚ إِنَّهُۥ هُوَ ٱلسَّمِيعُ ٱلْعَلِيمُ
>
> **Transliteration:** *Wa in janahū li-s-salmi fajnah lahā wa tawakkal 'alā Allāh, innahu huwa as-samī'u al-'alīm*
>
> **Translation:** "And if they incline to peace, then you incline to it [also], and rely upon Allah. Indeed, it is He who is the Hearing, the Knowing." (Surah 8:61, Sahih International)

This verse appears in Surah Al-Anfal (The Spoils of War), revealed after the Battle of Badr (624 CE). The context is critical: in the midst of military conflict, Allah commands Muslims to accept peace if enemies offer it.

**Linguistic analysis:**
- **Janaha:** To incline, lean toward, show willingness for
- **As-salm:** Peace, treaty, reconciliation (from same root as salaam)
- **Fajnah:** Imperative: "You also incline!" (command, not suggestion)
- **Tawakkal:** Trust, reliance upon God

**Classical tafsir (interpretation):**

**Ibn Kathir** (14th century): "If the enemy inclines toward peace by offering a truce or treaty, you must accept it... Allah commands accepting peace when offered, even in the middle of battle, because peace is always preferable to war."

**Al-Tabari** (9th century): "This verse abrogates permission to reject peace offers. Muslims are commanded to seek peace whenever possible and trust Allah for the outcome."

**Modern scholarly interpretation:**

Dr. **Ingrid Mattson** (former Islamic Society of North America president): "This verse establishes peace as the default state. War is permitted only defensively and must immediately cease when enemies offer peace. Muslims cannot continue hostilities merely for revenge or expansion."

**Historical application:**

Prophet Muhammad applied this principle in the **Treaty of Hudaybiyyah** (628 CE). Despite apparently unfavorable terms, he accepted peace when offered. The Quran later called this treaty "a clear victory" (Surah 48:1) because it enabled peaceful spread of Islam and eventual reconciliation.

**Modern relevance:**

This verse challenges any interpretation of Islam justifying perpetual warfare or rejecting peace negotiations. It mandates accepting genuine peace offers, trusting divine wisdom rather than human calculation of advantage.

**Surah 49:9-10 - Make Peace Between Believers**

> **Arabic:** وَإِن طَآئِفَتَانِ مِنَ ٱلْمُؤْمِنِينَ ٱقْتَتَلُوا۟ فَأَصْلِحُوا۟ بَيْنَهُمَا
>
> **Transliteration:** *Wa in ṭā'ifatāni mina al-mu'minīna iqtatalū fa-aṣlihū baynahumā*
>
> **Translation:** "And if two parties among the believers should fight, then make peace between them..." (Surah 49:9-10)

This verse addresses conflict *within* the Muslim community. Even when believers fight each other, the rest of the community is commanded to reconcile them.

**Key principles:**
1. **Conflict is acknowledged as reality:** Even believers sometimes fight
2. **Peacemaking is communal responsibility:** "Make peace" is plural imperative—everyone must work for reconciliation
3. **Justice precedes peace:** The verse continues: "...but if one oppresses the other, fight the oppressor until they return to Allah's command"
4. **Restoration, not revenge:** Once oppressor desists, make peace justly

**Application:** This establishes Islamic principle of *sulh* (reconciliation). Muslim communities historically developed sophisticated conflict resolution processes based on this verse—mediation, arbitration, restorative justice.

## Prophet Muhammad's Peace Treaties

The Quran's peace teachings weren't merely theoretical—Prophet Muhammad demonstrated them through historic peace agreements that became models for Islamic governance and diplomacy.

### The Treaty of Hudaybiyyah (628 CE)

**Historical context:**

In 628 CE, Prophet Muhammad and 1,400 Muslims journeyed from Medina to Mecca for pilgrimage (*umrah*). Meccans, still hostile to Islam, blocked their entry. Negotiations resulted in a controversial treaty.

**Treaty terms:**
1. Muslims would return to Medina *without* performing pilgrimage that year
2. Muslims could return next year for pilgrimage (three days only)
3. Ten-year truce between Muslims and Meccans
4. Arab tribes could ally with either side freely
5. **Controversial clause:** If a Meccan convert fled to Medina, Muslims must return them; but if a Muslim fled to Mecca, Meccans needn't return them (asymmetrical terms)

**Muslim reaction:**

Many companions were outraged by these "humiliating" terms. Umar ibn al-Khattab confronted Muhammad: "Are we not upon the truth and they upon falsehood? Why then should we concede in matters of our religion?"

Muhammad's response embodied Surah 8:61's principle: when enemies incline toward peace, accept it and trust Allah's wisdom.

**Divine vindication:**

Shortly after, Surah 48:1 was revealed: *"Indeed, We have granted you a clear victory."* How was this perceived defeat a victory?

**Results:**
1. **Peaceful contact:** The truce enabled previously hostile Meccans to interact with Muslims, leading to many conversions
2. **Freed resources:** No longer fighting Meccans, Muslims could address other threats (Khaybar campaign)
3. **Moral high ground:** Muslims demonstrated commitment to peace even from position of strength
4. **Eventual conquest:** Within two years, treaty violations by Meccan allies justified Muslim entry into Mecca—achieved *without bloodshed*

**Quranic commentary on treaty:**

Surah 48:18-21 celebrates this treaty, calling it God's victory and blessing those who pledged allegiance under a tree at Hudaybiyyah.

**Modern lesson:**

Hudaybiyyah demonstrates that:
- Peace is preferable even when terms seem disadvantageous
- Short-term "loss of face" may enable long-term victory
- Trusting divine wisdom over human calculation pays off
- Peaceful methods can achieve more than violence

### The Constitution of Medina (622 CE)

When Prophet Muhammad migrated to Medina (then called Yathrib), he drafted a historic document establishing a multi-religious confederation.

**Key provisions:**

1. **Mutual defense:** Muslims, Jews, and pagan Arabs would defend Medina together against external threats
2. **Religious freedom:** "To the Jews their religion, and to the Muslims their religion"
3. **Dispute resolution:** The Prophet would arbitrate disputes fairly
4. **Collective responsibility:** All tribes responsible for collective security and justice
5. **Prohibition of treachery:** No sheltering criminals or traitors

**Significance:**

This constitution is considered one of history's earliest written constitutions and a pioneering example of religious pluralism. It recognized:
- **Diversity:** Multiple religious communities could coexist under shared political framework
- **Citizenship:** Identity based on shared civic commitment, not just religious affiliation
- **Justice:** Fair arbitration regardless of religious background

**Islamic scholars' interpretation:**

Dr. **Muhammad Hamidullah** (20th-century Islamic scholar) called this document proof that Islam from its inception sought to create inclusive society respecting religious diversity within framework of justice and mutual security.

**Modern application:**

The Constitution of Medina provides precedent for:
- Multi-religious nations with Muslim majorities
- Citizenship rights for non-Muslims in Islamic contexts
- Interfaith cooperation for common good
- Contractual basis for political community

## Addressing Difficult Verses: Warfare in Context

**Critical question:** If the Quran teaches peace so extensively, why does it also contain verses about fighting and warfare? This requires honest, scholarly engagement.

### The Reality: Defensive Warfare Permission

The Quran does contain verses permitting warfare, but classical Islamic scholarship emphasizes these principles:

**1. Default is peace:** War is exception, not norm

**Surah 2:190**

> "Fight in the way of Allah those who fight you but do not transgress. Indeed, Allah does not like transgressors."

Key phrase: "those who fight *you*"—warfare is *defensive*, not aggressive.

**2. War must cease when enemies seek peace:** (Surah 8:61, discussed extensively above)

**3. Non-combatants are protected:**

Prophet Muhammad commanded: "Do not kill children, women, the elderly, or anyone in places of worship. Do not cut down trees or destroy crops." (Various hadith compilations)

International humanitarian law later codified similar principles.

**4. Proportionality required:**

Quran 2:194: "Whoever transgresses against you, respond in kind"—retaliation must be proportionate, not excessive.

### Historical Context Matters

**7th-century Arabia:** Early Muslims faced existential threats—persecution, exile, attempted genocide. Quranic warfare verses emerged in this context of survival.

Scholars distinguish:
- **Meccan verses** (610-622 CE): Emphasis on patience, forgiveness, non-retaliation (Muslims were weak minority)
- **Medinan verses** (622-632 CE): Permission for self-defense (Muslims had established community to defend)

This progression mirrors Christian history—early church was pacifist; later, when Christianity became state religion, "just war" theories emerged.

### The "Sword Verse" (Surah 9:5)

Often quoted out of context:

> "Kill the polytheists wherever you find them..."

**Full verse with preceding and following verses:**

Surah 9:4-6 clarifies this refers to *specific polytheist tribes* who broke treaties with Muslims. Verse 4 exempts those who kept treaties. Verse 6 commands: "If any polytheist seeks your protection, grant it to them so they may hear the word of Allah, then escort them to safety."

**Classical scholarly consensus (ijma):**

- Surah 9:5 is *specific* to historical context, not *universal* command
- Surah 8:61 (incline to peace) remains operative—not abrogated
- Warfare permissions are *conditional* on aggression, not *unconditional*

### Modern Islamic Peace Scholars

Contemporary Muslim scholars emphasize Quran's peace framework:

**Dr. Tariq Ramadan:** "The overwhelming message of the Quran is mercy, justice, and peace. Warfare verses must be read in their defensive context, not as perpetual mandates."

**Dr. Sherman Jackson:** "Muslims must recover the Quran's prioritization of peace. The 'clash of civilizations' narrative betrays Quranic ethics."

**Dr. Ingrid Mattson:** "Islam means peace through submission to God. Violence contradicts the name and nature of Islam itself."

### Deeper Parallels

**1. Same linguistic root:** *Salaam* (Arabic) and *Shalom* (Hebrew) come from identical Semitic root S-L-M, carrying the same semantic range—not coincidental but testament to shared origins.

**2. Jesus in both traditions:**

- **Bible:** Jesus teaches, "Blessed are the peacemakers" (Matthew 5:9), "Peace I leave with you" (John 14:27)
- **Quran:** Jesus (Isa) says, "Peace (*salaam*) on me the day I was born" (Surah 19:33)

Both traditions honor Jesus as teacher of peace, though understanding his nature differently (Christian: divine; Islamic: prophetic).

**3. Love of enemies:**

- **Bible:** Matthew 5:44
- **Quran:** Surah 60:8 permits kindness and justice toward non-hostile non-believers; Surah 41:34-35 teaches transforming enemies through goodness

**4. Reconciliation as worship:**

- **Islam:** Reconciliation between people equals charity (Surah 4:114)
- **Christianity:** "First be reconciled... then offer your gift" (Matthew 5:23-24)—reconciliation precedes ritual

### Why These Similarities Matter

**For interfaith dialogue:** Common ground exists for conversation. Christians and Muslims share more peace principles than differences.

**For challenging stereotypes:** Media often portrays "Christian peace vs. Islamic violence." Reality: both traditions teach peace as ideal and permit defensive warfare as exception.

**For collaborative peacemaking:** When Christians and Muslims work together for justice, reconciliation, and nonviolence, they're acting consistently with both traditions' deepest values.

## How Muslims Practice Peace Today

Quranic peace teachings aren't relics of the past—they actively shape Muslim practice worldwide.

### Daily Practice: Greeting of Peace

Every day, hundreds of millions of Muslims greet each other:

**"As-salamu alaykum"** (Peace be upon you)
**Response:** "Wa alaykum as-salaam" (And upon you be peace)

This isn't empty formula—it's *du'a* (supplication), invoking divine blessing of peace upon another person. The practice:
- Reminds Muslims that peace is central to identity
- Creates micro-moments of blessing throughout the day
- Includes everyone—Muslim or non-Muslim (Prophet Muhammad greeted all people this way)

### The Five Daily Prayers: Peace Invocations

Every *salah* (ritual prayer) concludes with *tasleem*, turning head right and left while saying:

**"As-salamu alaykum wa rahmatullah"** (Peace and mercy of Allah be upon you)

This greeting is directed to:
- Angels recording deeds
- Fellow worshippers (if praying in congregation)
- All creatures
- Symbolically, all of creation

Five times daily, Muslims ritually invoke peace—embodying Quran's vision of believers as peace-bringers.

### Sulh: Islamic Reconciliation Practices

Based on Surah 49:9-10 and prophetic example, Muslim communities developed *sulh* (reconciliation) processes:

**Traditional sulh elements:**
1. **Intervention by respected third party:** Elder, scholar, or community leader mediates
2. **Private dialogue:** Disputants meet confidentially
3. **Focus on restoration:** Goal is healed relationship, not punishment
4. **Compromise:** Both parties expected to give ground
5. **Public reconciliation:** If successful, community celebrates and witnesses reconciliation

**Modern applications:**
- **Informal mediation:** Mosques often have conflict resolution committees
- **Formal arbitration:** Some Muslim-majority countries incorporate sulh into legal systems
- **Restorative justice:** Some Western Muslim communities use sulh principles in youth justice programs

## Conclusion: Salaam as Islamic Essence

From the greeting *"As-salamu alaykum"* to the name of God *As-Salaam* to the destination of paradise *Dar al-Salaam*, peace permeates Islamic teaching and practice.

### What We've Learned

**The Quran explicitly teaches peace:**
- Peace as divine attribute (Allah is *As-Salaam*)
- Peace as command (incline to peace when offered)
- Peace as practice (reconciliation between people)
- Peace as future hope (paradise as abode of peace)

**Historical example reinforces teaching:**
- Treaty of Hudaybiyyah chose peace over apparent advantage
- Constitution of Medina created multi-religious peaceful community
- Prophet's life modeled mercy and forgiveness

**Difficult verses require context:**
- Warfare permissions are defensive, not aggressive
- Peace remains default; war is exception
- Classical scholarship emphasizes Quran's overwhelming peace ethic

**Common ground with other faiths:**
- Salaam and Shalom share linguistic and conceptual roots
- Quran and Bible teach remarkably similar peace principles
- Jesus is honored as peace teacher in both traditions
